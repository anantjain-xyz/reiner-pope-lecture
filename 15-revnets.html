RevNets and Reversible Transformers

A 1973 cipher construction, dropped into a residual block, lets you train a transformer that stores essentially zero activation memory in exchange for one extra forward pass.

training memory activations reversible networks long context
TL;DR

Standard backpropagation has to keep every layer's activations in memory until the corresponding gradient arrives, which is why activation memory in transformer training scales as $O(L \cdot B \cdot S \cdot H)$ — number of layers times batch times sequence times hidden dimension — and quickly dwarfs parameters at long sequence. A reversible network rearranges each layer so that, given the output, you can recompute the input exactly. The construction is identical to a Feistel cipher round from cryptography: split the residual stream into two halves, push one through an arbitrary function, add it to the other. You can then throw activations away during the forward pass and reconstruct them on demand during the backward pass, paying roughly one extra forward pass for the privilege. This is the dual of the key-value cache trick: there you trade memory for compute, here you trade compute for memory. Reversibility is the most aggressive form of activation checkpointing and it composes with sharding (ZeRO) and with selective checkpointing, so the three techniques are complementary rather than competing.

Why this matters

Reiner Pope ended the Dwarkesh Patel blackboard interview by drawing a Feistel cipher. Two parallel arrows down the board, a function $f$ in the middle, an XOR symbol off to the side. He pointed at it and said: this is also a reversible neural network. Then he erased the board and the interview was over. It is a strange place to leave things — a cryptographic primitive from 1973 sitting next to a discussion of MatX silicon and key-value caches — but the point is sharp. The whole interview revolves around a single tradeoff: memory versus compute. Most of the conversation is about buying memory with compute (KV caches, MQA, paged attention). Reversible networks go the other direction: they buy compute time back as memory savings during training. If your bottleneck is HBM rather than FLOPs, that is precisely the trade you want.

For training large models, activation memory is the dominant first-order cost. The parameters of a 70-billion-parameter model in bfloat16 take 140 GB. The activations during a single training step at sequence length 16k and batch size 4 take, naively, several hundred GB more. Every long-context training run, every long-video model, every billion-token retrieval-augmented setup runs into this wall. Gradient checkpointing softens it; reversible layers pretty much eliminate it. By the end of this tutorial you should be able to read the RevNet and Reformer papers fluently, derive the inversion equations for any reversible block, and have a checklist for deciding whether your next training run should use reversible layers or not.

Beginner Where activation memory comes from, and the Feistel trick

The activation memory bill

Backpropagation is a chain-rule walk back through the network. To compute the gradient of the loss with respect to a layer's weights, you need three things: the gradient flowing in from above, the layer's stored input, and (sometimes) intermediate quantities the layer computed on its forward pass. The first comes from above, the second and third have to come from the forward pass — which means they have to be kept in memory from the moment the layer fires until the moment its gradient arrives. Schematically:

For a transformer, the dominant terms in the activation footprint of one layer are the residual-stream tensor (one or two copies, depending on what gets saved for layer-norm), the query/key/value projections, the attention probabilities (which are $B \cdot \text{heads} \cdot S \cdot S$ — quadratic in sequence length), and the feed-forward intermediate (which is $4 H$ wide). Putting it together, the activation memory for an entire training step scales like

$$M_{\text{act}} \;\approx\; L \cdot B \cdot S \cdot H \cdot c_1 \;+\; L \cdot B \cdot \text{heads} \cdot S^2 \cdot c_2$$

where $c_1$ and $c_2$ are small constants that depend on which activations the framework saves. For a 32-layer model with $H = 4096$, $\text{heads} = 32$, $B = 4$, $S = 8192$, in bfloat16, the linear part alone is about $32 \cdot 4 \cdot 8192 \cdot 4096 \cdot 2 \cdot 5 \approx 43$ GB if you keep five tensors per layer. The quadratic attention term adds another 64 GB. Compare this to the parameters, which are about 14 GB in bfloat16 for a 7-billion-parameter model. Activations dominate. This is the squeeze that reversibility was invented to solve.

The Feistel trick

Horst Feistel was a German-American cryptographer at IBM who, in 1973, published a paper called "Cryptography and Computer Privacy." In it he laid out the structure that would later become the basis of DES, Lucifer, Blowfish, and a long lineage of block ciphers. The structural insight is one drawing:

Write out the round equations. With $\oplus$ standing for any group operation that has an inverse (XOR over bits, addition over reals):

$$L' = R \qquad R' = L \oplus f(R, K)$$

The miracle is the inverse. Given $(L', R')$ and knowing $f$ and $K$, we want to get back $(L, R)$:

$$R = L' \qquad L = R' \ominus f(L', K)$$

Two lines. No matrix inversion, no Newton iteration, no learned decoder. We just rearranged the equations. And critically, $f$ can be anything we like — the inversion does not call $f^{-1}$, only $f$ itself. Feistel's contribution was realizing that you can build an invertible structure out of a non-invertible component, by separating the role of "the thing being scrambled" from "the thing doing the scrambling." Cryptographers care about this because it lets them use S-boxes and hashes (which are deliberately hard to invert) without losing decryptability. Neural-network engineers care about it because it lets them use ReLU networks (which are not invertible — half the inputs map to the same zero) without losing the ability to recover earlier activations.

From cipher round to ResNet block

A standard ResNet block is $y = x + F(x)$ where $F$ is a small stack of convolutions. To make it reversible, we apply the Feistel pattern: split the channel dimension in half, route one half through $F$, add it to the other. Replace XOR with real addition. The result is the RevNet block of Gomez, Ren, Urtasun, and Grosse (2017):

$$y_1 = x_1 + F(x_2) \qquad y_2 = x_2 + G(y_1)$$

The two functions $F$ and $G$ are arbitrary — typically each one is a small residual sub-block. Inverting:

$$x_2 = y_2 - G(y_1) \qquad x_1 = y_1 - F(x_2)$$

Two lines again. Notice the order matters: you have to recover $x_2$ first because the formula for $x_1$ uses it. But once you have the recipe, you can reconstruct any layer's input from its output by paying for one forward call to $G$ and one to $F$ — exactly one layer's worth of compute. That is the deal. You no longer have to store $(x_1, x_2)$ on the way down; on the way back up you can recompute it.

A worked numerical example

Let's actually run the equations. Take $x_1 = 3$, $x_2 = 5$, $F(u) = u^2 + 1$, $G(u) = 2u$. Forward:

  • $y_1 = 3 + F(5) = 3 + 26 = 29$
  • $y_2 = 5 + G(29) = 5 + 58 = 63$

So the block sends $(3, 5) \mapsto (29, 63)$. Now invert from $(29, 63)$:

  • $x_2 = 63 - G(29) = 63 - 58 = 5$ ✓
  • $x_1 = 29 - F(5) = 29 - 26 = 3$ ✓

$F$ here is not invertible — $F(5) = F(-5) = 26$ — and yet the block is. The trick is that we never had to invert $F$; we only had to evaluate it. That is the whole idea, dressed up in real numbers.

Intermediate RevNet, the Reformer, and the inverse pass in detail

The RevNet paper in one page

Gomez, Ren, Urtasun, and Grosse (NeurIPS 2017) took the additive coupling layer from Dinh, Krueger, and Bengio's NICE (Non-linear Independent Components Estimation) flow paper and asked a different question: forget density estimation, what if we just want to skip storing activations during training? They showed that ResNet-101 and ResNet-164, replaced block-for-block with a RevNet of the same parameter count, achieved essentially identical accuracy on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet. The activation memory dropped to a constant independent of depth. Compute went up by roughly 33% because each backward step does an extra forward call to recover activations.

One subtle point: a RevNet block has to keep its tensor shape constant. Standard ResNets downsample with stride-2 convolutions, which throw information away and break invertibility. RevNets handle this by inserting non-reversible "transition" blocks at downsampling boundaries — those layers do store activations the old-fashioned way, but they are a small minority. The bulk of the depth is reversible and pays the constant memory price.

The backward pass, written out

Here is the full algorithm for a single reversible block during backprop. Inputs: outputs $(y_1, y_2)$ and gradients $(\bar{y}_1, \bar{y}_2)$. Output: inputs $(x_1, x_2)$, gradients $(\bar{x}_1, \bar{x}_2)$, and parameter gradients.

The actual paper writes the gradient propagation in closed form using Jacobians, but the implementation in PyTorch or JAX is exactly the structure above: invert, then re-run the small subgraph through autograd. The crucial property is that the recomputation is local — you only need the immediate output to recover the immediate input. So you can stream backward through the network one block at a time, never holding more than two layers' worth of activations live at once.

Reformer: reversibility plus locality-sensitive attention

Kitaev, Kaiser, and Levskaya (ICLR 2020) wrote down the obvious next step: replace each transformer block with a Feistel pair, where one half is the attention sublayer and the other is the feed-forward sublayer. The reversible transformer block becomes:

$$y_1 = x_1 + \text{Attention}(x_2) \qquad y_2 = x_2 + \text{FFN}(y_1)$$

This is a beautiful structural match. The two sublayers of the transformer are already separated by a residual connection, so you do not even need to invent new modules — you just rearrange the wiring. The Reformer paper combined this with two other tricks: locality-sensitive hashing (LSH) attention to bring the $O(S^2)$ attention cost down to $O(S \log S)$, and chunked feed-forward layers to avoid materializing the full $4H$-wide intermediate. Together these meant the paper could train transformers on sequences of 64,000 tokens on a single accelerator.

One nuance worth flagging: Reformer-style reversibility composes multiplicatively with the locality-sensitive hashing. The LSH brings attention down to $O(S \log S)$ in compute, while reversibility brings it down to $O(1)$ in activation memory per layer. They attack different bottlenecks. If your sequence length is short, you do not need either; if it is medium, LSH is enough; if it is genuinely long and the model is deep, you want both.

Parameter and compute overhead

RevNet blocks at first appear to double parameters because they have two functions $F$ and $G$ where a ResNet block has one. The standard fix is to halve the channel count of each so that the total parameter count matches. Once you do that, RevNets are roughly parameter-equivalent to ResNets of the same depth and width, with the same arithmetic intensity. The pure cost is the extra forward pass per backward step. The fact that reversibility achieves $O(1)$ memory at only $+33\%$ compute is the key marketing claim. Plain recompute-from-checkpoint at the same memory footprint would cost $O(L)$ compute. Reversibility wins because the inversion operation costs the same as the forward operation — one matmul through $F$ and one through $G$ per block. There is no asymptotic blowup.

Reversible Vision Transformers

Mangalam, Fan, Li, Wu, Xiong, Feichtenhofer, and Malik (CVPR 2022) ported the Reformer recipe to vision. Their Reversible ViT replaces each ViT block with a Feistel pair using multi-head attention as $F$ and the MLP as $G$. They report up to 15.5× memory reduction at the same accuracy, and up to 14× larger batch size for ViT-L. The interesting wrinkle is that hierarchical vision transformers (MViT, Swin) have downsampling stages that change the feature dimension; those stages cannot be reversible, so the savings shrink to about 52% for Rev-MViT — still substantial, but bounded by how much of the depth is invertible.

The vision setting is friendlier than language for reversibility because vision sequences are short and dense, so the activation memory of the residual stream dominates the activation memory of the attention probabilities. Once you remove the residual stream cost, you are basically done. In language models with long context, attention probabilities themselves are a major contributor — which is why FlashAttention (which avoids materializing them) and Reformer's LSH attention (which also avoids the full $S \times S$ tensor) tend to be deployed alongside reversibility.

Three memory-saving techniques compared

Reversibility is one of three orthogonal tools for taming training memory. They attack different parts of the budget and they compose.

The choice of which to use is dictated by what is actually saturating your HBM. If parameter and optimizer state are the killer (typical for 100B+ models on 80 GB GPUs), ZeRO is the lever. If activation memory is the killer (typical for long-context fine-tuning, video models, or anywhere $S^2$ matters), then checkpointing or reversibility is the lever. If you can stomach the engineering complexity, reversibility wins on the Pareto curve. If you cannot, $\sqrt{L}$ checkpointing is a one-line change in PyTorch (torch.utils.checkpoint) that gets you 80% of the way there.

The connection to normalizing flows

The Feistel block has a parallel life in generative modeling. Dinh, Krueger, and Bengio's NICE (2014) and Real NVP (2017) used the same additive coupling structure to build invertible probability density models. In that setting the goal is different: you want $p(x) = p(z) \cdot |\det J|^{-1}$ for some change of variables $x = T(z)$, and the determinant is tractable iff $T$ has triangular Jacobian — which an additive coupling layer does, by construction. RevNet uses the same architecture but does not care about the determinant; it only cares about the inverse map.

This is a fun example of cross-pollination: cryptography in 1973 noticed that additive coupling preserves invertibility regardless of the inner function. Generative modeling in 2014 noticed that the same structure has a tractable Jacobian. Memory-efficient training in 2017 noticed that the same structure lets you skip activation storage. Three different fields, three different applications, one block diagram.

Reversible attention and recent work

The vanilla Reformer construction wraps attention into one half of a Feistel pair, but attention itself is not reversible — the softmax destroys information. More recent work has explored truly reversible attention: variants that include the attention input on both sides of the residual stream so that the operation can be undone exactly. Examples include RevTransformer variants in the Macaron family and the Reversible Vision Transformer line.

There is also a growing literature on combining reversibility with FlashAttention-style fused kernels. The challenge is that FlashAttention recomputes attention probabilities during the backward pass already (for memory reasons), so the marginal benefit of reversibility on the attention sublayer specifically is smaller than on the MLP sublayer. Some libraries (xFormers, MemSAVE-style activation managers) expose selective reversibility on a per-sublayer basis, letting you trade more carefully across the whole network.

When reversibility is the right tool

A short checklist before you reach for reversible layers:

  • Is activation memory the actual bottleneck? Run with checkpointing first; if HBM is still tight, escalate to reversibility.
  • Is your model deep? The savings scale with depth. A 6-layer toy model does not benefit much.
  • Are you willing to write custom backward passes? Most autograd frameworks need a hand-rolled reversible block to get the memory savings; the off-the-shelf torch.utils.checkpoint does not deliver them.
  • Is the model architecture mostly residual stream + sublayers? Pre-norm transformers and ResNets are easy. Architectures with information-destroying steps (downsampling, pooling, anything that changes dimension) need extra engineering.
  • Do you care about exact reproducibility? Reversibility is an exact reconstruction in real arithmetic, but with bfloat16 you can accumulate small numerical drift across many block inversions. RevNet papers report this is not a practical accuracy issue, but it can confuse debugging.

Open questions and what to read next

  • Does reversibility scale to truly trillion-parameter language models with 1M+ context, or does the bfloat16 numerical drift in inversion eventually hurt? The 2024-25 generation of long-context papers will tell us.
  • Can attention itself be made reversible without the half-residual hack? Some work on associative-memory transformers and state-space models points this way.
  • How does reversibility interact with quantized training (FP8, MX formats)? Inversion is a subtraction, which is sensitive to catastrophic cancellation in low precision.
  • Is there an automatic compiler pass that picks the best memory-vs-compute tradeoff per layer (selective checkpointing + selective reversibility) given a memory budget?
  • What is the right reversible analogue of mixture-of-experts? The expert routing is not reversible by construction, so naive MoE breaks the property.
