Character.AI's Inference Stack

How a chat product served 20,000 queries per second by shrinking its KV cache more than 20×. The recipe, and how to assemble your own.

inference KV cache attention quantization
TL;DR

Character.AI's June 2024 inference blog is the canonical public reference for shrinking the transformer KV cache. Four ideas stacked: multi-query attention, interleaved local-global attention at a 5:1 ratio of sliding-window to full-context layers, cross-layer KV sharing so only roughly one layer in three computes new K and V, and int8 quantization across weights, activations, and the cache itself. Add a 95%-hit-rate stateful cache that persists per-conversation KV between turns and you have a 33× cost-per-message reduction since 2022 and a roughly 95-98% drop in resident KV footprint. Google's Gemma 2 and Gemma 3 ship the same local-global trick (1:1 in Gemma 2, 5:1 in Gemma 3). The pattern is now the default for chat-shaped workloads.

Why this matters

Character.AI's "Optimizing AI Inference at Character.AI" blog post is a useful public reference for driving the KV cache down. In mid-2024, Character.AI was one of the few non-hyperscaler companies running a chat product at very high volume — about 20,000 queries per second, which they noted was roughly 20% of Google Search traffic — and they wrote down the full recipe. Most public KV-cache work benchmarks isolated tricks. Character.AI's blog is one of the few places that shows how the tricks compose in production.

The connection to the interview's themes is simple. To serve a chat product profitably, the bottleneck isn't flops, it's bytes. Specifically the bytes of the KV cache, because batch size is gated by how many sequences' caches fit in HBM. In the interview, Reiner briefly notes that the "Character AI kind of model" — where the global context is shared across all layers — also showed up in the Gemma models. After reading this, you should be able to look at a model architecture spec, estimate its KV footprint, and put together a reduction stack that hits a target cost-per-token for your workload.

Beginner What is the KV cache and why does it dominate cost?

A decoder-only transformer generates one token at a time. To produce token $t+1$ it computes attention over all previous tokens $1 \ldots t$. If we recomputed every previous token's K (key) and V (value) projections at every step, decoding would be $O(n^2)$ work per token. The fix is the KV cache: after the first time we compute K and V for position $i$, we store them in HBM and reuse them on every later step. Decoding then becomes $O(n)$ per step in compute, but it costs memory.

How much memory? The standard formula for a multi-head attention (MHA) model is:

$\text{KV bytes} = 2 \cdot L \cdot n_h \cdot d_h \cdot s \cdot b \cdot \text{bytes per element}$

where $L$ is the number of layers, $n_h$ is the number of attention heads, $d_h$ is the per-head dimension, $s$ is sequence length, $b$ is batch size, and the leading 2 is because we store both K and V. The factor "bytes per element" is 2 for bfloat16, 1 for int8, and so on.

For a typical 70B-parameter MHA model, $L=80$, $n_h=64$, $d_h=128$. At sequence length $s=8{,}192$ in bfloat16 that is $2 \cdot 80 \cdot 64 \cdot 128 \cdot 8{,}192 \cdot 2 \approx 21$ GB of KV cache per concurrent user. An H100 has 80 GB. So even a perfectly optimized server could only fit three or four users at full context — long before flops become the limit.

This is the cost-per-message problem in one paragraph. Inference cost is dominated by the lowest concurrency you can fit on a chip; concurrency is gated by KV bytes; so anything that reduces KV bytes per user multiplies your throughput almost directly. Character.AI's claim that they cut cost-per-message by more than 33x since 2022, ending up at less than one cent per hour of conversation, is mostly a story of attacking the KV cache from four directions at once.

One clarification on terminology before we move on. The "context length" people quote on a model card is the maximum $s$ the model was trained or fine-tuned to support. The KV cache scales with the actual $s$ you have stored, not the maximum. A 1M-context model serving a 200-token chat reply uses 200 tokens of KV cache, not a million.

Intermediate The four-layer attack on KV cache size

Character.AI's blog credits three architectural changes for ">20x KV cache reduction without quality regression": multi-query attention, hybrid attention horizons (local-global mixing), and cross-layer KV sharing. We will walk each one and then show how they compose multiplicatively.

1. Multi-query attention (MQA)

In standard multi-head attention, every attention head has its own K and V projection. If you have $n_h=64$ heads, you store 64 separate K and V tensors per layer per token. Multi-query attention, introduced by Noam Shazeer in 2019, simply collapses all heads' K and V to a single shared K and a single shared V — only Q stays per-head. The grouped-query attention (GQA) paper by Ainslie et al. in 2023 generalized this to $g$ groups, with MHA at $g=n_h$ and MQA at $g=1$.

For a model with 64 heads, MQA gives an 8x to 64x reduction in per-token KV bytes (8x is the typical comparison against an LLaMA-2-style 8:1 GQA baseline, 64x is the comparison against full MHA). Character.AI uses MQA "in all attention layers." There is a small quality tax — MQA models are slightly worse at recall on long contexts than MHA — but for chat workloads with short individual turns the trade is overwhelmingly favorable.

2. Hybrid attention horizons (local-global)

The second move: most attention layers do not need to see the whole conversation. They only need a sliding window of the recent past. A few "global" layers preserve the ability to attend to anything older. Character.AI interleaves sliding-window attention with global attention in a roughly 5:1 ratio, with a sliding window of about 1,024 tokens. Five out of every six layers only ever store K and V for the last 1,024 tokens; the sixth layer is the only one with a full-length KV cache.

The KV-cache impact depends on context length. At sequence length $s$ much longer than the window $w$, the local layers contribute only $w$ tokens of cache instead of $s$. With $s = 8{,}192$ and $w = 1{,}024$, each local layer caches $1/8$ as much, and overall the layer stack caches roughly

$\frac{5}{6} \cdot \frac{w}{s} + \frac{1}{6} = \frac{5}{6} \cdot \frac{1{,}024}{8{,}192} + \frac{1}{6} \approx 0.27$

or about a 3.7x reduction. At $s = 32{,}768$, the same calculation gives a 5.3x reduction. The longer the context, the more this trick helps.

Why does this work without breaking quality? Empirically, most of what a transformer does at most layers is local: nearby-token syntactic and semantic composition. Only a few layers need the long range. This same insight underlies the Mistral 7B sliding-window paper (where every layer is local but the receptive field grows with depth, akin to a CNN) and the Longformer / Big Bird family. Character.AI's contribution was to be very public about the fact that you can take this all the way to a 5:1 ratio in a production model.

3. Cross-layer KV sharing

The third move is even more aggressive. The blog explains it as: "We tie the KV cache across neighboring attention layers. For global attention layers, we tie the KV cache of multiple global layers across blocks." In practice, only a fraction of layers actually compute fresh K and V. The remaining layers reuse the K and V tensors from a designated source layer. The independent academic version of this is "Cross-Layer Attention" (CLA) by Brandon et al. (May 2024), who report that CLA2 (sharing across pairs of layers) gives an additional 2x KV reduction at MQA-equivalent quality.

The key design choice is which layers act as producers and which as reusers. Character.AI ties together the local layers in groups, and separately ties together the global layers across blocks (since global layers are scarce but heavy, sharing them across every block of 6 is especially impactful). Independent reproductions, like Niranjan Kumar's nanoGPT port, find that "first 3 local layers share, last 2 local layers share, all global layers share" gives a clean Pareto-optimal point.

Worked KV-cache size calculation

Let's make this concrete with a hypothetical 13B-parameter chat model: $L = 40$ layers, $n_h = 32$ heads, $d_h = 128$ per head, sequence length $s = 8{,}192$, batch size $b = 1$. We will compute KV bytes per concurrent user under each successive optimization.

Baseline (MHA, bf16, no sharing):

$2 \cdot 40 \cdot 32 \cdot 128 \cdot 8{,}192 \cdot 1 \cdot 2 = 5.37 \text{ GB}$

Step 1: MQA. One K head and one V head per layer instead of 32. Reduction: 32x.

$5.37 \text{ GB} / 32 = 168 \text{ MB}$

Step 2: Hybrid attention horizons (5:1 local-global, $w=1{,}024$). Local layers shrink from $s=8{,}192$ to $w=1{,}024$ tokens stored. The 33 local layers each shrink 8x. The 7 global layers stay at full length.

$168 \text{ MB} \cdot \left(\tfrac{33}{40} \cdot \tfrac{1}{8} + \tfrac{7}{40}\right) = 168 \text{ MB} \cdot 0.278 = 46.7 \text{ MB}$

Step 3: Cross-layer KV sharing (~3x further reduction). Roughly one third of layers actually store fresh K and V. Conservatively assume 3x.

$46.7 \text{ MB} / 3 = 15.6 \text{ MB}$

Step 4: int8 quantization on the KV cache. bf16 → int8 is exactly 2x.

$15.6 \text{ MB} / 2 = 7.8 \text{ MB}$

Combined reduction: $5{,}370 / 7.8 \approx 690\text{x}$ on per-user KV cache. The blog claims "more than 20x" from architecture alone, which is what you get from MQA + cross-layer + horizons compared to a strong GQA baseline (not MHA). On top of that, int8 and stateful caching deliver the rest. The headline operational claim — that resident KV cache footprint is reduced by roughly 95-98% — is consistent with these compounded factors.

Advanced Stateful caching, int8, and where the strategy breaks

4. Stateful caching across turns

The four-layer architectural attack reduces per-user KV bytes. The fifth move reduces recomputation. Chat conversations have long shared prefixes: the same character card, system prompt, and prior turns repeat across every step. If you keep that prefix's KV cache around between turns, you only need to compute K and V for the new tokens.

Character.AI describes "an efficient system for caching attention KV on host memory between chat turns, achieving a 95% cache rate." On a cache hit, you do almost no prefill work — just re-attend. The implementation lives in CPU/host RAM (which is far larger and cheaper than HBM) and streams the relevant prefix KV back to GPU on demand. Because their architecture has a small KV footprint, more conversations fit in host RAM, which is the only reason a 95% hit rate is even achievable.

5. Int8 quantization, end to end

Character.AI quantizes weights, activations, and the KV cache to int8 — and crucially, they "natively train [models] in int8 precision" rather than applying post-training quantization (PTQ). Native int8 training avoids the activation-distribution drift that makes PTQ fail above a few billion parameters. This halves KV cache bytes (bf16 → int8, the final 2x in our worked example) and roughly halves activation bandwidth on supported hardware.

The follow-up post in late 2024, "Optimizing AI Inference at Character.AI (Part Deux)", goes deeper on the kernel side: a "fused int8 attention" kernel that uses int8 tensor cores for the QK matmul (with float-precision dequant of the scores) and bf16 cores for the attention-weighted V matmul. They report "up to 10% in prefill" and "up to 30% in decoding" speedups over their prior Triton baseline, with "9.3x" reduction in kernel time at large batch sizes when packing multiple MQA query heads into one threadblock to amortize the WGMMA tile size on Hopper.

How Gemma 2 and Gemma 3 generalize the pattern

Google's Gemma 2 (2024) and Gemma 3 (2025) ship the local-global pattern as a default. Gemma 2 alternates every other layer (1:1, sliding window 4,096). Gemma 3 went further: 5 local layers per 1 global layer, sliding window 1,024, supporting up to 128K context on the 4B+ models. That ratio is identical to Character.AI's. Reiner notes in the interview that the "Character AI kind of model" — sharing global context across all layers — also showed up in the Gemma models, suggesting the same trick was validated at hyperscale.

Where the strategy breaks: very long single-turn contexts

The Character.AI stack is tuned for a chat workload. That means many concurrent conversations, each with a moderate-length context that grows turn by turn, and a high prefix-cache hit rate. Three regimes break this trade-off:

  • Single very long contexts (think: a 500K-token codebase summarization). The 5:1 local-global ratio still helps, but the 1/6 of layers that retain full attention now dominate, and there is no inter-turn cache to amortize prefill. You want either ring-attention-style sequence-parallel decoding or a model trained with a much higher fraction of global layers.
  • Workloads with no shared prefix (random user-generated retrieval at scale). Stateful caching collapses to baseline. You give back most of the cost win.
  • Tasks that need long-range recall in many layers (coding agents, exact retrieval). MQA + sliding windows degrade exact-match tasks more than pure NLU benchmarks suggest. The MixAttention paper from Databricks (Sep 2024) explored which layers can be local without quality loss and found the answer is workload-dependent.

The practical implication: design your KV reduction stack for your workload. A chat product can adopt the full Character.AI playbook. A long-document summarizer should keep more global layers and lean on prefill-time tricks. A code-completion product probably wants closer to MHA in the late layers and saves bytes via cross-layer sharing instead.

A design checklist for your own stack

  1. Estimate per-user KV bytes for your model and your $p_{99}$ context length. If it exceeds 1% of HBM, you have a concurrency problem before you have a flops problem.
  2. Adopt MQA or GQA-2/4 unless your workload has a strong long-range-recall requirement. MQA gives the largest single multiplier.
  3. Pick a local-global ratio matched to your context-length distribution. 1:1 is conservative (Gemma 2), 5:1 is aggressive (Gemma 3, Character.AI). Sweep on a held-out eval that includes long-recall tasks.
  4. Add cross-layer KV sharing only after measuring quality on the above. CLA2 is usually safe; CLA3+ trades quality for memory.
  5. If your traffic has shared prefixes (system prompts, character cards, RAG context), stand up host-RAM stateful caching. vLLM's automatic prefix caching and SGLang's RadixAttention both implement this with one config flag.
  6. Quantize the KV cache to int8 (or even fp8 / int4) once everything else is in place. If you control training, train natively in low precision rather than PTQ.

Open questions and what to read next

  • How low can the global-layer fraction go? Gemma 3 is at 1/6. Some research has explored 1/8 or even fully-local with periodic re-injection. The quality cliff is workload-dependent and not well characterized publicly.
  • Can KV sharing be learned end-to-end? Cross-Layer Attention is currently a fixed pattern. A trainable router that decides which layer reuses which producer's KV could give per-input adaptivity.
  • What replaces local-global in Mamba/SSM hybrids? Models like Jamba and Hymba mix attention with state-space layers. Their KV-cost story is different — and arguably better — but the public production data is sparse.
  • How does the Character.AI playbook compose with paged attention and continuous batching? vLLM's PagedAttention reduces fragmentation; the Character.AI tricks reduce raw bytes. They are orthogonal but the interaction with prefix caching is subtle.
  • What is the right precision floor for the KV cache? int8 is now standard. fp8 (E4M3) is showing up on Hopper. int4 with per-group scales is plausible. Where does quality break first — KV, weights, or activations?
