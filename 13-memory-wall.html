The Memory Wall: HBM, DDR, Flash, and Why Memory is Half of CapEx

After this page you will be able to read SemiAnalysis-style memory market notes fluently and reason quantitatively about why a 2025-era data center spends as much on DRAM as on logic silicon.

memory hierarchy HBM DDR CXL supply chain
TL;DR

Compute throughput on accelerators has been doubling roughly every 2 years; memory bandwidth has been doubling every 3 to 4 years. The cumulative gap is the memory wall, and in 2025 it manifests as physical scarcity: hyperscalers are spending close to 50% of capital expenditure on memory, every wafer slot at SK Hynix, Samsung, and Micron is allocated, smartphone and PC manufacturers are paying triple-digit price hikes for double-data-rate (DDR) modules, and architecture decisions (long-context attention, mixture of experts, key-value caches) are driven less by FLOPs than by what fits in high-bandwidth memory (HBM). This tutorial walks the full hierarchy from registers to network-attached storage, explains why HBM is so expensive to make, tabulates HBM1 through HBM4, and works a back-of-the-envelope calculation on what it takes to host a 5-trillion-parameter model.

Why this matters

In the Dwarkesh Patel blackboard interview, Reiner Pope nodded along to Dylan Patel's claim that hyperscalers are spending roughly half of their capital expenditure (CapEx) on memory. That is a startling number. Five years ago a graphics processing unit (GPU) was, for accounting purposes, a logic chip with some attached memory. Today, in dollar terms, an NVIDIA HGX H200 server is closer to a memory product with attached compute. The shortage has rippled outward: Xiaomi's chief financial officer warned in late 2025 that smartphone bills of materials would jump because every dynamic random-access memory (DRAM) wafer being diverted to HBM is a wafer not making low-power double-data-rate memory (LPDDR) for phones. Spot prices for DDR5 quadrupled between September 2025 and Q1 2026.

This is not a transient supply blip. It is the long-prophesied memory wall — a structural mismatch between how fast logic shrinks (Moore's law plus packaging tricks) and how fast off-chip memory bandwidth grows (limited by pin count, signaling physics, and the geometry of stacking). For an ML infrastructure engineer in 2026, understanding this wall is table stakes. It explains why mixture-of-experts (MoE) architectures dominate frontier models, why long-context attention is so expensive, why companies like Groq and Cerebras built strange-looking chips with hundreds of megabytes (or tens of gigabytes) of static random-access memory (SRAM) on-die, and why every serving stack now has a tiered key-value (KV) cache that spills to non-volatile memory express (NVMe) flash.

This tutorial is the longest in the series for a reason: memory is now the dominant story of AI hardware economics.

Beginner The memory hierarchy and the original wall

What is the memory wall?

In 1995 William Wulf and Sally McKee published a four-page note in Computer Architecture News titled Hitting the Memory Wall: Implications of the Obvious. Their argument was almost trivially simple. Suppose central processing unit (CPU) speed doubles every 18 months and main memory speed doubles every 7 years. Then the ratio of CPU-to-memory speed grows exponentially, and within a decade or two any program that touches memory more than rarely will spend essentially all its time waiting. Wulf and McKee called the resulting plateau the memory wall. Caches, they noted, only delay the inevitable: as the gap widens, even a 99% cache hit rate stops being good enough.

Thirty years later the geometry has shifted but the inequality is worse, not better. Amir Gholami and colleagues at Berkeley put numbers on it in their 2024 paper AI and Memory Wall: peak server hardware floating point operations per second (FLOPS) have grown 3.0× every 2 years over the last two decades, while DRAM bandwidth has grown only 1.6× every 2 years and interconnect bandwidth only 1.4×. Compounded over ten years that is roughly a 240× compute gain against a 10× bandwidth gain.

The memory hierarchy in a modern accelerator

To understand why this gap is binding, it helps to walk through the memory hierarchy from the perspective of a single tensor operation on a GPU. Each level trades capacity for bandwidth and latency.

Each rung is roughly an order of magnitude bigger and an order of magnitude slower than the one above. The job of the compiler, kernel author, and serving system is to keep hot data near the top. When that fails — when a tensor has to be re-fetched from HBM repeatedly because it does not fit in SRAM, or when a model spills out of HBM into host DDR — performance falls off a cliff.

A back-of-the-envelope worked example

Let us make this concrete. Suppose you want to host a 5-trillion-parameter dense Transformer. How many H100 GPUs (80 GB HBM each) does it take just to hold the weights?

That is just the weights. Now add a key-value (KV) cache for serving 1,000 concurrent users at a 200,000-token context. A reasonable rule of thumb for a frontier-scale model is a KV-cache footprint of about 1 megabyte per token at bf16 (this depends on hidden size, number of layers, and number of KV heads, but the order of magnitude holds for big MoE models with grouped-query attention). Then:

1,000 users × 200,000 tokens × 1 MB = 200 TB of KV cache.

That is 2,500 H100s of HBM, just for the cache, on top of the weights. This is why frontier inference clusters look the way they do: tens of thousands of accelerators, mostly providing memory rather than compute, with extremely aggressive batching, paged attention, prefix caching, and offload tiers (CPU DDR, then NVMe via systems like LMCache) to claw back capacity. Halve the precision, halve the context, share prefixes across users, or move to mixture of experts (where only a fraction of weights are active per token), and the numbers become tractable. Without those tricks the math does not close.

Intermediate HBM up close, DDR, CXL, and flash tiers

What HBM actually is

High-bandwidth memory is a specific JEDEC (Joint Electron Device Engineering Council) standard for stacked DRAM. Instead of mounting DRAM dies on a printed circuit board (PCB) and routing thousands of signal traces across the motherboard, HBM stacks 4 to 16 DRAM dies vertically on top of a "logic base die" and connects them with through-silicon vias (TSVs) — copper-filled holes etched straight through each silicon wafer. The whole stack sits on a silicon interposer right next to the GPU die, sharing the same package.

Three things make this expensive. First, etching TSVs through DRAM wafers needs special equipment and damages yield — a 12-Hi stack with 99% per-layer yield is only 88% good in aggregate. Second, the silicon interposer that carries the wide 1024-bit (HBM3) or 2048-bit (HBM4) bus from the stack to the GPU is itself a piece of silicon larger than the logic chip. Third, the stack must be assembled with sub-micron alignment and bonded with either thermal-compression non-conductive film (TC-NCF) or SK Hynix's mass-reflow molded underfill (MR-MUF). All three steps run on capacity that simply does not exist in the volumes hyperscalers want.

The "bytes per FLOP" column is the key health metric. Roofline analysis says you are memory-bound when the operation's arithmetic intensity (FLOPs per byte) is below this threshold. A naive matrix-vector product is around 2 FLOPs per byte. So decoder-style autoregressive inference, which is dominated by matrix-vector products on the KV cache, is fundamentally bandwidth-limited on every accelerator in the table — and the ratio is getting worse, not better, when you switch to FP4. This is why so many recent ideas (speculative decoding, MoE, FlashAttention, prefix caching) are really bandwidth tricks dressed up as algorithms.

Host DDR and CXL: the next tier down

Below HBM is a slower, larger world. A modern dual-socket server holds 1 to 8 terabytes of DDR5 at speeds of 4800 to 6400 megatransfers per second (MT/s). A single DDR5 channel delivers about 38 to 50 GB/s; a 12-channel server CPU therefore offers about 0.5 TB/s of host memory bandwidth — roughly an order of magnitude less than HBM, but at one-tenth the cost per gigabyte and ten times the capacity per server.

The catch is that getting from a GPU to host DDR has historically cost a PCIe round-trip, with bandwidth limited to ~64 GB/s on PCIe Gen5 x16. Compute Express Link (CXL) is the new fabric that aims to close this gap. CXL runs over the same PCIe physical layer but adds cache coherence and memory semantics, so a host can attach a "memory expander" — basically a box of DDR with a CXL controller — and present it to the CPU as additional, slower, NUMA-style memory. CXL 3.0 raises bandwidth to 128 GB/s bidirectional per link and adds switching, so multiple hosts can share a memory pool.

For LLM serving the relevant use case is KV cache offload: spill the cold portion of a long-context conversation from HBM to a CXL pool, then back into HBM when the user resumes. Penguin Solutions and Compute Express Link Consortium publications report 3.8× speedups on KV transfer compared with 200 Gbit/s remote direct memory access (RDMA) and up to 50% improvement in memory utilization on Llama-class models. CXL is not yet ubiquitous in production, but every hyperscaler is experimenting.

Flash as a memory tier: NAND, NVMe, LMCache

Below host DDR sits NAND flash, organized into solid-state drives (SSDs) attached over the NVMe protocol. A modern PCIe Gen5 NVMe drive does about 14 GB/s sequential read at 30 to 60 terabytes of capacity. That is 1000× the capacity of an HBM stack but 1000× slower. Three years ago no one would have called flash a memory tier for inference. The KV-cache problem has changed that.

Systems like LMCache (open source, integrated with vLLM and SGLang) and NVIDIA Dynamo treat the KV cache as a tiered store with policies that match the access pattern: hot prefixes stay in HBM, warm conversations spill to host DDR or CXL, and cold conversation history (or shared system prompts) lands on local NVMe or even networked storage. Because KV-cache reads are large, sequential, and predictable, NAND latency of ~50 microseconds per access is tolerable when overlapped with prefill compute. NVIDIA reported in early 2026 that this kind of offload allows a single H100 to serve roughly 10× more concurrent users on long-context workloads than HBM alone permits.

Advanced Why HBM is constrained, why memory is half of CapEx, and the architectural escapes

Why HBM supply is the bottleneck of the AI build-out

There are exactly three companies in the world capable of producing HBM at scale: SK Hynix, Samsung, and Micron. SK Hynix ships roughly 50 to 55% of the market, Samsung 35 to 40%, Micron 5 to 10%. NVIDIA's HBM contracts go almost entirely to SK Hynix because of its yield advantage on the MR-MUF process; Samsung has spent two years trying to qualify HBM3E for NVIDIA and has had bumpy yields; Micron came in late but is now a credible second source.

Why three? Because HBM requires the intersection of three rare capabilities: leading-edge DRAM cells (1α or 1β nodes, currently only at the Big Three plus China's CXMT), TSV-capable wafer fabs with the deposition and etch tools to make 30,000 vias per square millimeter through hardened DRAM, and advanced packaging (interposers, bonding, micro-bump pitch). And HBM ramping is slow: each new generation takes 18 to 24 months from sampling to volume. The 2024 to 2026 demand surge from training (xAI Colossus, OpenAI Stargate, Meta Hyperion, Google's TPU v6/v7, Anthropic's Project Rainier) hit a supply curve that simply could not be made vertical.

SemiAnalysis quantified the squeeze in late 2025: HBM consumes roughly 23% of all DRAM wafer capacity globally. Because HBM uses about 3× more silicon per bit than DDR5 (the dies must be thinner, and TSVs eat area), every bit of HBM produced removes about 3 bits of consumer DDR/LPDDR from the market. The result has been the most violent DRAM price cycle in 40 years: DDR5 spot quadrupled from September 2025 to Q1 2026, DDR4 jumped 14× since April 2025, and Xiaomi, Samsung's mobile group, and Dell all pre-announced product price increases of 15 to 25% for 2026.

The half-of-CapEx number, decomposed

Take a single NVIDIA HGX H200 server: 8 GPUs × 141 GB HBM3E = 1.13 TB of HBM. At late-2025 contract prices of roughly $20 per gigabyte for HBM3E (more for HBM3E 12-Hi spot), that is on the order of $22,000 of HBM packaged into about $300,000 of server. Add 2 TB of DDR5 server memory at spiked 2026 prices and the host memory adds another $20,000 to $30,000. Storage (NVMe, networked) contributes single-digit percent. Total: HBM and DDR together are 15 to 20% of bill of materials. That sounds less than 50%, but the 50% figure that Dylan Patel and Reiner Pope discuss is the marginal cost of memory in a hyperscaler's CapEx run-rate — including the long-term offtake agreements that hyperscalers sign with HBM vendors years in advance, including the price premium charged on top of NVIDIA's own GPU margin (which itself is partly a markup on HBM), and including the network and storage memory that fills out a data center. By that broader accounting, memory really is competing with logic for the top of the budget.

Architectural escapes: PIM, wafer-scale, on-chip SRAM

If you cannot widen the pipe, the only way out is to shorten it — bring compute closer to memory or fit memory closer to compute. Three families of attempts are in production or near production.

Processing in memory (PIM). Samsung's HBM-PIM (commercial name Aquabolt-XL) embeds small "programmable computing units" inside the HBM stack itself. The PCUs perform reductions, activations, and small matrix-vector ops without sending the data out to the GPU. Demonstrations on a Xilinx Alveo target showed a 2.5× speedup and 60% energy reduction on speech-recognition workloads. The catch: PIM only helps when your kernel is bandwidth-bound and trivially parallel within a stack. It is not a general escape. AMD has shipped some experimental PIM prototypes, and the JEDEC HBM4 spec leaves room for future PIM variants.

Large on-chip SRAM (Groq). Groq's Language Processing Unit (LPU) takes the opposite approach: skip HBM entirely. Each LPU contains roughly 230 megabytes of on-die SRAM with about 80 TB/s of internal bandwidth — 25× the bandwidth of an H100, but at 1/350th the capacity. Groq's bet is that for serving (not training) you can shard a model across thousands of LPUs and stream tokens through a deterministic pipeline at very low latency. The trade-off: you pay enormous up-front silicon area for SRAM, and you cannot efficiently host models that do not fit in your fleet's aggregate SRAM.

Wafer-scale (Cerebras). Cerebras takes the SRAM idea even further. The Wafer-Scale Engine 3 (WSE-3) is a single chip the size of a dinner plate, containing 900,000 cores and 44 GB of distributed on-wafer SRAM at over 21 PB/s of aggregate bandwidth. A Cerebras CS-3 system can hold a 70-billion-parameter model entirely on-wafer at FP16, with no off-chip DRAM in the critical path. It is the most extreme answer to the memory wall in the wild today. The trade-off: the wafer is expensive to manufacture, and the architecture is best suited to dense models.

Sparsity and architecture co-design. Frontier models have been responding to the memory wall not just at the silicon level but at the architecture level. MoE models like DeepSeek-V3 and Llama 4 store hundreds of billions of total parameters but activate only ~37B per token, so the bandwidth-bound decode step touches a fraction of HBM. Multi-query and grouped-query attention shrink the KV cache by 4× to 8×. State-space models (Mamba) drop quadratic attention entirely, replacing it with an O(1)-memory recurrence. None of these are pure memory plays, but their popularity is largely a memory-wall response.

Reading SemiAnalysis-style memory analysis fluently

If you got this far, the following kinds of statements should now parse without footnotes:

"SK Hynix is sold out of HBM3E 12-Hi through Q3 2026. Samsung qualified its HBM3E for NVIDIA in November but is yielding 50%, leaving Micron to fill the gap. CoWoS-L capacity at TSMC remains the binding constraint on Rubin shipments, not HBM4 itself."

That sentence packs three constraints: a memory vendor (HBM3E 12-Hi from SK Hynix), a packaging house (TSMC's chip-on-wafer-on-substrate, large variant), and a downstream customer (NVIDIA Rubin). Each of these is a separate ramp curve with its own yields, contracts, and lead times, and the AI build-out only goes as fast as the slowest of them.

Open questions and what to read next

  • Will hybrid bonding (wafer-to-wafer copper-to-copper) replace micro-bumps, allowing 20+ Hi HBM stacks and dropping z-height? SK Hynix has demos; volume is unclear.
  • Will custom HBM ("HBM4 base die") let hyperscalers and accelerator vendors specialize the logic die for compute-in-memory? Marvell and Broadcom have announced partnerships.
  • Is processing-in-memory ever going to escape benchmarks and into production training pipelines?
  • Will CXL 3.x see deployment beyond niche pilots, or will RDMA over Ethernet (RoCE) win the disaggregated-memory layer?
  • Does the architecture community settle on a memory-aware Pareto frontier (MoE, Mamba, sparse attention) or do dense Transformers re-emerge once HBM4/HBM5 ease the squeeze?
  • If HBM truly becomes a national-strategic resource, how will the U.S./Korea/China supply geometry rebalance?
