Pipeline Scheduling: From Bubbles to DualPipe

Why "pipelining is not wise" was true for years, and what the modern schedules do to make it almost wise again.

distributed training parallelism scheduling
TL;DR

Pipeline parallelism (PP) splits a model layer-wise across devices so each device only stores a slice of the weights and activations. The simplest schedule, GPipe, fills the pipeline forward then drains backward, leaving a triangular idle region called the "bubble" with fraction $(P-1)/(M+P-1)$ for $P$ stages and $M$ micro-batches. Modern schedules — 1F1B, interleaved 1F1B, Zero Bubble, and DualPipe — progressively reorder forward and backward operations, split the backward pass into input-gradient (B) and weight-gradient (W) halves, and feed micro-batches from both ends of the pipeline. They cut the bubble to near zero at the cost of extra activation memory and scheduler complexity. Pipeline parallelism is fundamentally a memory-capacity tool, not a speedup tool: you reach for it when the model no longer fits on one rack of accelerators.

Why this matters

In the Dwarkesh interview, Reiner Pope drew a Gantt chart on the blackboard with stages stacked vertically and time running horizontally, then traced out the staircase of forward computations followed by a mirrored staircase of backward computations. He shaded in the empty triangles at the top-right and bottom-left — the pipeline bubble — and quoted Ilya Sutskever's blunt assessment: "pipelining is not wise." His point was not that pipelining is useless. It is that the bubble is a real, structural cost during training, even though it disappears during inference (where you can simply keep launching new requests onto the front of the pipe). The forward-backward dependency in training creates a hard synchronization point that leaves accelerators idle.

That was the framing as of GPipe in 2018. Since then, a sequence of papers — PipeDream, Megatron-LM's interleaved schedule, Zero Bubble Pipeline, and DeepSeek V3's DualPipe — have eroded the bubble down to nearly zero in synchronous training. Understanding the progression is one of the cleanest case studies in ML systems research: each paper identifies a single structural assumption from the previous one, breaks it, and trades a little memory or scheduling complexity for utilization.

This page walks the chain. By the end you should be able to draw any of these schedules on a whiteboard from memory, derive the bubble fraction for each, and explain to a colleague why DeepSeek's DualPipe diagram has micro-batches flowing in both directions.

Beginner Pipelining a model and the bubble

What pipeline parallelism is

Imagine a 100-layer transformer. If the weights, optimizer states, and per-token activations all fit on a single accelerator, you would just train it there with data parallelism: every device holds a copy of the whole model and they synchronize gradients after each step. But large models do not fit. A 671B-parameter MoE model like DeepSeek V3 has somewhere in the neighborhood of 1.3 TB of fp16 weights alone, before optimizer states and activations. No single accelerator — and not even a single 8-GPU server — can hold it.

You need to split the model. There are three canonical axes of split:

  • Tensor parallelism (TP). Split each individual matrix multiply across devices. Communication is heavy (an all-reduce per layer) but latency is hidden inside one server's NVLink fabric.
  • Pipeline parallelism (PP). Split the model along the layer dimension. Stage 0 holds layers 1-25, stage 1 holds layers 26-50, and so on. Devices only talk at stage boundaries, sending activations forward and gradients backward. Communication is small and tolerates higher latency, so PP is the natural choice for spanning racks or even data-center floors.
  • Data parallelism (DP). Replicate the whole pipeline and feed different data to each replica.

PP wins on bandwidth requirements because the cross-stage messages are just one activation tensor (forward) or one gradient tensor (backward) per micro-batch. That is why production training stacks usually look like "TP inside a server, PP across servers, DP across replicas." The downside is that PP introduces sequential dependencies between stages, and managing them is the entire subject of this page.

The naive schedule and where the bubble comes from

The dumbest possible schedule: run the entire forward pass on stage 0, ship activations to stage 1, run the entire forward pass there, and so on through stage $P-1$. Then run the entire backward pass in reverse. This is "naive model parallelism." It is correct but catastrophic — at any moment exactly one of the $P$ stages is busy, so device utilization is $1/P$. With four stages you have used 25% of your hardware.

The GPipe insight (Huang et al., 2018) is to chop the global batch into $M$ smaller "micro-batches" and stream them through the pipeline. As soon as stage 0 finishes the forward pass on micro-batch 1, it starts on micro-batch 2 while stage 1 picks up micro-batch 1. The pipeline fills like water in a trough. After a "fill" period of $P-1$ steps, all stages are busy. Then forward is done, the loss is computed, and the backward pass drains in mirror image — fill, steady state, drain.

Computing the bubble fraction

Let $t_f$ be the wall-clock time of one forward micro-batch on one stage and $t_b$ be the time of the corresponding backward. Assume all stages are balanced. The total useful work in one training step is

$T_{\text{ideal}} = M \cdot (t_f + t_b)$ per stage,

but the actual end-to-end time on the slowest stage is the time to fill, run, and drain:

$T_{\text{actual}} = (M + P - 1) \cdot (t_f + t_b)$

The bubble is the gap:

$\text{bubble fraction} = \dfrac{T_{\text{actual}} - T_{\text{ideal}}}{T_{\text{actual}}} = \dfrac{P-1}{M + P - 1}$

This is the canonical formula. Note that it does not depend on $t_f$ or $t_b$ at all — only on the number of stages and micro-batches.

Worked example: P = 4 and M = 8

Plug in $P = 4$ and $M = 8$:

$\dfrac{4 - 1}{8 + 4 - 1} = \dfrac{3}{11} \approx 0.273$

So about 27% of stage-time is wasted in the bubble. To halve that you would have to roughly double $M$. With $M = 16$ you get $3/19 \approx 16\%$. With $M = 32$ you get $3/35 \approx 9\%$. The original GPipe paper recommended $M \geq 4P$, which gives a bubble fraction of $(P-1)/(5P-1) \approx 1/5$ for large $P$ — workable, but still a meaningful slice of the cluster's flops.

Why you cannot just crank M up forever

The catch is activation memory. In GPipe, every micro-batch that has finished forward but has not yet started backward must keep its activations in memory so that the backward pass can recompute gradients. With $M$ micro-batches in flight, stage 0 in particular hangs onto activations for nearly all $M$ of them at peak. Memory at stage $i$ scales like $O(M \cdot a / P)$ where $a$ is the activation size of the full model, since each stage holds $1/P$ of the layers but $M$ live micro-batches. Activation checkpointing helps but does not eliminate the issue.

Worse, $M$ is also bounded below. You need $M \geq P$ for the pipeline to ever reach steady state, and you need the global batch size $G = M \cdot \mu$ (where $\mu$ is the micro-batch size) to be small enough that gradient noise scaling laws still produce useful updates. Frontier training runs already operate at the upper edge of useful batch sizes, so simply scaling $M$ to drown out the bubble is not free. This is why the schedule itself has to be smarter.

Intermediate 1F1B, weight stashing, and interleaving

PipeDream and 1F1B

Narayanan et al.'s PipeDream (2018, then a 2019 SOSP paper) made two contributions. The first is a different schedule, called one-forward-one-backward (1F1B). The second is a memory bookkeeping trick called weight stashing.

The 1F1B observation is simple: in GPipe, stage 0 sits idle while stages 1, 2, 3 are still finishing forward, and again while they are doing backward. But there is no fundamental reason stage 0 has to wait for the entire forward sweep to complete before starting backward on something. As soon as any backward gradient comes back to stage 0, it can process it. So PipeDream interleaves: each stage launches just enough forwards to fill the pipe, then enters a steady state where it strictly alternates one forward, one backward.

Surprisingly, 1F1B has the same bubble fraction as GPipe: $(P-1)/(M+P-1)$. The fill and drain regions still contain $P-1$ steps of idle stages each. What 1F1B saves is memory. At any moment, each stage holds activations for at most $P$ in-flight micro-batches (instead of up to $M$ in GPipe), because backward consumes activations as soon as they are produced. This decouples activation memory from $M$, freeing you to push $M$ as large as you like to shrink the bubble without paying memory cost.

Weight stashing

1F1B introduces a subtle correctness issue. Consider stage 0. By the time it does the backward pass for micro-batch 1, it has already done the forward pass for micro-batches 2, 3, 4, 5. If at some point stage 0's optimizer steps the weights, then the backward for micro-batch 1 would use different weights than its forward used. Gradients computed against weights that differ from the forward pass produce biased updates.

PipeDream's weight stashing trick is to keep multiple versions of the weights around — specifically, one version for each in-flight micro-batch — and use the matching version on the backward pass. Each stage stashes up to $P$ weight copies. This restores forward-backward consistency at the price of a few times the parameter memory on each stage. PipeDream's original variant ran the optimizer asynchronously (different stages could be stale), but its synchronous descendants — including all schedules in production today — use 1F1B with a single weight version per step and a flush at the end of each global batch, which is what the diagram above depicts.

Interleaved 1F1B (Megatron-LM)

Narayanan et al. revisited the schedule in the 2021 Megatron-LM paper and noticed that you can make the bubble smaller still by taking more stages — virtually. Instead of giving each device one contiguous block of layers, give it $v$ non-contiguous blocks (called "model chunks" or "virtual stages"). For $v = 2$ and 4 physical devices, device 0 holds layers 1-12 and 49-60, device 1 holds layers 13-24 and 61-72, and so on. The pipeline depth becomes $vP$ instead of $P$, but each chunk is $1/v$ the size, so each forward and backward operation is now $t_f/v$ and $t_b/v$.

The bubble formula becomes:

$\text{bubble fraction}_{\text{interleaved}} = \dfrac{1}{v} \cdot \dfrac{P-1}{M+P-1}$

You pay for it with extra cross-stage communication — there are $v$ times as many activation send/recv operations per micro-batch — and a slightly more complex scheduler. With $v = 4$ on a $P = 8$ system, you cut the bubble by a factor of 4.

Where this leaves us

By 2021 the basic playbook for production training was: tensor-parallel within a server, pipeline-parallel across servers using interleaved 1F1B with $v$ chosen so that the bubble was a small single-digit percentage, and data-parallel across replicas. This was the recipe used to train GPT-3-class models. The interview with Reiner reflects this period: pipelining is "not wise" in the sense that you would never choose it for speed, but you accept it because you have no other way to reach a 100B+ parameter model.

Advanced Zero Bubble and DualPipe

Splitting the backward pass

Zero Bubble Pipeline (Qi et al., 2024) makes a structural observation that earlier papers had glossed over. The backward pass of a layer actually does two distinct things:

  • B (input gradient). Compute $\partial L / \partial x$, the gradient with respect to the input activation. This must be sent upstream to the previous stage so it can do its backward pass. This is the part of backward that has a hard data dependency on the next stage downstream.
  • W (weight gradient). Compute $\partial L / \partial \theta$, the gradient with respect to the weights. This stays on the local stage; nothing else in the pipeline needs it. It only has to finish before the optimizer step at the end of the global batch.

For a typical transformer block, B and W each take roughly the same amount of time, so $t_b = t_B + t_W$ with $t_B \approx t_W \approx t_f$. Treating B and W as separately schedulable operations is the entire trick of Zero Bubble.

The Zero Bubble paper presents three variants of increasing aggressiveness:

  • ZB-H1. Same memory as 1F1B. Reorders B and W so that some bubble cells are filled by W. Shrinks the bubble by roughly a factor of 2.
  • ZB-H2. Allows extra in-flight micro-batches at the cost of more activation memory. Achieves zero bubble in steady state.
  • ZB-V. A "V-shaped" placement that achieves zero bubble with the same memory as 1F1B, by dovetailing the chunk assignment so that down-stream and up-stream operations fit together without slack.

The bubble formula for ZB1P (the synchronous, 1F1B-memory variant of Zero Bubble) is

$\text{bubble}_{\text{ZB1P}} = (P-1)(t_F + t_B - 2 t_W)$

compared with the 1F1B baseline of $(P-1)(t_F + t_B)$. If $t_B \approx t_W$, the residual bubble is just $(P-1)(t_F - t_W) \approx 0$ since $t_F \approx t_W$ for typical transformers. Empirically the paper reports up to 23% throughput gain over 1F1B at the same memory budget, and 31% if memory is relaxed.

DualPipe: feeding micro-batches from both ends

DeepSeek's V3 technical report (Dec 2024) and the open-source DualPipe release (Feb 2025) push one further structural break: a bidirectional pipeline. Imagine two copies of the model laid end-to-end on the same set of devices. Stage 0 holds the first chunk of model copy A and the last chunk of model copy B. Stage 1 holds the second chunk of A and the second-to-last chunk of B. Micro-batches flow left-to-right through copy A and right-to-left through copy B, simultaneously. Each device is now busy with two pipelines running in opposite directions, and the fill/drain regions of one pipeline overlap with the steady state of the other.

Two things are happening simultaneously in this diagram:

  1. Bidirectional micro-batches. Half of the micro-batches enter at stage 0 and exit at stage $P-1$. The other half enter at stage $P-1$ and exit at stage 0. Because the fill and drain shapes are mirror-symmetric, when one direction is filling the other is draining, and vice versa. The bubble shrinks from $(P-1)$ stages worth to $(P/2 - 1)$.
  2. Compute-communication overlap. Each forward chunk in DeepSeek's MoE architecture decomposes into attention, all-to-all dispatch (routing tokens to experts), MLP (expert FFN), and all-to-all combine. For backward, attention and MLP are further split into B (input grad) and W (weight grad) as in Zero Bubble. DualPipe pairs forward and backward chunks of opposite-direction micro-batches and overlaps their attention and MLP compute with each other's all-to-all communication. The all-to-all is the dominant cost in MoE training, so hiding it inside the compute of another micro-batch is a huge win.

From DeepSeek V3's Table 2, the bubble formula is:

$\text{bubble}_{\text{DualPipe}} = \left(\dfrac{P}{2} - 1\right)(t_{F\&B} + t_B - 3 t_W)$

where $t_{F\&B}$ is the duration of an overlapped forward-and-backward chunk pair. The activation memory cost grows as $(P + 1) \times$ baseline, compared with $P \times$ for 1F1B and ZB1P. The price is essentially holding two copies of model parameters on each rank — a non-trivial cost, but tractable when the model is dominated by sparsely-activated MoE experts (as in DeepSeek V3) where parameter memory is amortized over a large number of devices anyway.

Interaction with MoE and tensor parallelism

Pipeline scheduling does not happen in isolation. Two interactions matter most for frontier training:

  • MoE and all-to-all. Mixture-of-experts models route each token to a small subset of experts. If experts are sharded across devices (expert parallelism), every forward layer needs an all-to-all communication to dispatch tokens and another to combine the outputs. These all-to-alls dwarf the activation transfers between pipeline stages and dominate communication cost. DualPipe's "overlap" mechanism is fundamentally about hiding these MoE all-to-alls behind compute from the opposite-direction micro-batch.
  • Tensor parallelism. TP sits inside each pipeline stage. As long as each TP region behaves like a single fused operation from the pipeline's perspective, none of the schedules above care whether a stage is implemented as one device or eight. In practice you choose TP big enough that activations fit, then PP big enough that weights fit, then DP for the rest of the cluster.

Why pipelining is "not wise" but unavoidable

Even with DualPipe-class schedules, pipeline parallelism does not give you a speedup. It gives you the ability to fit a model that would not otherwise fit. Here is the framing Reiner emphasized: every stage you add to the pipeline is one more sequential step in the dependency chain. The flops per second per device is, at best, equal to what you would get on a smaller problem with no pipelining. The bubble erosion across GPipe → 1F1B → ZB → DualPipe is about not losing performance to scheduling — it is not about gaining anything. You reach for pipelining when you have run out of TP scaling within a server (limited by NVLink), run out of DP because your batch size is already too large, and you still need to hold a model that does not fit in one server's HBM. At that point, you accept the bubble (now nearly zero) and the scheduler complexity, because nothing else gets you a 600B-parameter model.

This is exactly why "pipelining is not wise" but everyone uses it anyway. Inference is different — there is no hard forward-backward boundary, so you can keep launching new requests and the steady-state utilization approaches 100%. That is what Reiner meant when he said the bubble disappears at inference.

Open questions and what to read next

  • Is there anything left to squeeze out of synchronous schedules? ZB-V and DualPipe come within a few percent of zero bubble. The remaining inefficiencies are dominated by load imbalance (some layers genuinely take longer than others) and communication latency. Are there better automated schedule-search methods?
  • Asynchronous pipelining for RL. Reinforcement learning fine-tuning has a different dependency pattern (rollout, scoring, update). Can pipeline schedules be redesigned for that workload, or is data parallelism enough?
  • Cross-data-center pipelining. If activations are small enough to send across long-distance links, you could in principle put pipeline stages in different data centers. What is the bandwidth-latency frontier?
  • Compiler integration. Today's schedules are still hand-written. JAX's Shardy, PyTorch's Pipelining, and torchtitan are converging on a notion of automatic schedule synthesis. Will it be possible to specify a schedule declaratively and let the compiler emit the bubble-optimal version?
  • Pipelining for non-transformer architectures. Recurrent and state-space models have different forward-backward dependency graphs. Do the same schedules apply?
