6ND — Where the Famous Floating-Point Operation Count Comes From

Estimate the FLOPs for any training run on a napkin. And know why the constant is 6.

scaling laws compute transformers back-of-the-envelope
TL;DR

Training a transformer takes about $C \approx 6ND$ FLOPs, where $N$ is parameters and $D$ is training tokens. The 6 splits cleanly: 2 FLOPs per parameter per token in the forward pass, then 4 in the backward pass (2 for the input gradient, 2 for the weight gradient). Inference is just the forward half: $2ND$. RL lands anywhere from $2ND$ to $6ND$ depending on what fraction of generated tokens you backpropagate through. The formula counts only the linear-layer matrix multiplies. It ignores attention's quadratic term and uses active parameters for MoE. For most pretraining runs at moderate context length, the approximation is accurate to within a few percent.

Why this matters

Halfway through the interview, Reiner scribbles "$6ND$" on the board, then immediately offers a sequel: RL is "two to six $ND$" depending on whether you backpropagate through every rollout, and inference is just $2ND$. That small family of formulas does a lot of work. It's how you convert "how big is my model" into "how much does it cost to train," and through that into dollars on the GPU bill.

$6ND$ shows up under most of the field's load-bearing numbers. Kaplan used it to derive the original scaling laws in 2020. Hoffmann used it to derive Chinchilla's "20 tokens per parameter" in 2022. Epoch AI uses it to track total training compute. Every lab uses it to plan capacity: chips times peak FLOP/s times utilization times wall-clock time, divided by $6ND$, equals "how big a model can I train this quarter." Cluster procurement decisions, scaling-law extrapolations, Twitter arguments about whether some new release is GPT-4-class — they all silently rely on this constant.

Ask a typical practitioner where the 6 comes from and you usually get a hand-wave about backward being twice forward. That's right but incomplete. The constant decomposes cleanly into 2 + 2 + 2: three matrix multiplies you can draw on a whiteboard. Once you have the picture, you can re-derive the formula for any architecture, see where MoE changes it, see why attention is usually negligible (and when it isn't), and see why "machine FLOPs" can be six times "model FLOPs" without anyone making a mistake. This page is the picture.

Beginner Counting FLOPs in a matrix multiply

What is a FLOP?

A FLOP — floating-point operation — is one arithmetic operation on real-valued numbers: an add, a subtract, a multiply, or a divide. (Compares, integer ops, and bookkeeping like indexing don't count.) FLOPs are an abstract measure of work; FLOP/s (FLOPs per second) is a measure of speed. An NVIDIA H100 advertises around 1000 trillion FLOP/s in bfloat16 with sparsity off; our entire job is to count how many FLOPs a training run requires, then divide by chip-seconds to budget the run.

One subtlety: hardware engineers usually count a fused-multiply-add (FMA) — the operation $a \cdot b + c$ — as two FLOPs even though the chip implements it as a single instruction. This convention is universal in the ML literature, so we'll use it. Adam Casson notes the alternative bluntly: "matmuls consist of a 2 FLOP multiply-accumulate operation (one multiply and one add) for each element in a weight matrix."

The matrix-vector multiply

Take a weight matrix $W$ of shape $(m, n)$ and an input vector $x$ of shape $(n, 1)$. The output $y = Wx$ has shape $(m, 1)$. Each entry of $y$ is a dot product of one row of $W$ with $x$, which costs $n$ multiplies and $n-1$ adds — about $2n$ FLOPs. There are $m$ output entries, so the total cost is roughly $2mn$ FLOPs.

That's the only equation you need to internalize: a matrix multiply costs 2 FLOPs per entry of the matrix per vector you push through it. The matrix has $mn$ entries, which equals the number of parameters in this little linear layer. So the cost per input is exactly $2 \times (\text{parameters})$.

From a layer to a model

A transformer is, at its core, a stack of these matrix multiplies. Add up the parameter counts of every linear layer (the query / key / value / output projections inside attention, and the up / down projections inside the feed-forward block) and you get a number $N$. Push one token through the network and the total FLOP cost — adding up all those 2-times-parameters contributions — is approximately $2N$.

That gives the forward-pass rule:

FLOPs per token (forward) ≈ 2 × N

Run a batch of $D$ tokens through and you spend $2ND$ FLOPs in the forward pass. This is the entire cost of inference (per token generated), and it's where the famous "$2N$ FLOPs per token" rule for inference comes from.

What about everything that isn't a matrix multiply?

Real transformers also have layer norms, GELU/SwiGLU activations, residual additions, softmax, and rotary position embeddings. These are pointwise or near-pointwise: they touch each activation a constant number of times. Their cost scales with the number of activations, not with parameters times tokens, and that turns out to be tiny. As Kaplan et al. note in a passing aside that has launched a thousand back-of-the-envelope calculations: "nonlinearities, biases, normalizations, and residuals are not counted as they turn out to be negligible."

Concretely, layer norm costs about $5d_{\text{model}}$ FLOPs per token per occurrence (compute mean, variance, normalize, scale, shift). With two layer norms per layer and, say, 32 layers, that's $\sim 320 d_{\text{model}}$ FLOPs per token. For a 7-billion-parameter model with $d_{\text{model}}=4096$, the layer norms cost $\sim 1.3$ million FLOPs per token, while the linear layers cost $\sim 14$ billion. Layer norm is one part in ten thousand. We round it away.

Intermediate Deriving 6ND for a full transformer

The backward pass: where the 4 comes from

Backpropagation is, mathematically, the chain rule applied via dynamic programming. For each linear layer $y = Wx$ during training, we eventually receive an upstream gradient $\frac{\partial L}{\partial y}$ (call it $g_y$) from the layer above. To pass gradients on, we need to compute two new things:

  1. Input gradient $\frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = W^\top g_y$. Shape: $(n, 1)$. Cost: another matrix multiply through the $W$-sized matrix, so $\approx 2mn$ FLOPs.
  2. Weight gradient $\frac{\partial L}{\partial W} = g_y \, x^\top$. Shape: $(m, n)$, summed across the batch. Cost: $\approx 2mn$ FLOPs per example.

So the backward pass through one linear layer costs $4mn$ — twice the forward pass. Stacking all the layers and summing parameters gives $4N$ FLOPs per token in the backward pass, or $4ND$ for $D$ tokens.

Adding forward and backward together:

training FLOPs per token  =  2N (forward)  +  4N (backward)  =  6N

Multiply by $D$ tokens, and you get the headline number:

$$C_{\text{train}} \approx 6 N D$$

Why the first layer of the network is the only exception

For the very first layer (the embedding), there is no "input gradient" to pass back — the input is a discrete token id, not a continuous tensor. So in principle, the embedding layer only needs the $2mn$ weight-gradient computation in the backward pass, not the full $4mn$. In practice the embedding parameters are often treated separately from "$N$" anyway (we'll see why below), and the discrepancy is small. Most authors fold this into the rounding error.

What's inside a transformer layer, term by term

Let's count parameters for a single decoder-style layer with hidden size $d$ (also written $d_{\text{model}}$) and feed-forward expansion factor 4 (so the MLP hidden dimension is $4d$):

SublayerParametersForward FLOPs / token
Q, K, V projections$3 d^2$$6 d^2$
Attention output projection$d^2$$2 d^2$
Feed-forward up ($d \to 4d$)$4 d^2$$8 d^2$
Feed-forward down ($4d \to d$)$4 d^2$$8 d^2$
Layer norms (×2)$\sim 4d$$\sim 10d$
Per-layer total (matmuls only)$12 d^2$$24 d^2$

Stack $L$ layers and the parameter count is $N \approx 12 L d^2$. Forward FLOPs per token are $\approx 24 L d^2 = 2N$, exactly as advertised. Note how layer norms barely register: $4d$ versus $12 d^2$, with $d$ in the thousands.

(SwiGLU and GLU variants used in modern Llama-style models swap "two $d \to 4d$ matrices then activate" for "three $d \to \tfrac{8}{3}d$ matrices with a gated activation". The total parameter count per layer is still $\approx 12 d^2$ by construction, and the 6ND rule still holds. Architecture choices that change activation shape do not change parameter count, by deliberate design.)

Worked example: 7B parameters, 1T tokens

Llama-2-7B has $N = 6.74 \times 10^9$ parameters. Suppose we train it on $D = 1 \times 10^{12}$ tokens (roughly the original Llama-2 recipe was 2T; we'll do 1T for round numbers). Then:

C ≈ 6 × N × D
  = 6 × 6.74e9 × 1e12
  = 4.04e22 FLOPs
  ≈ 40 zettaFLOPs (ZFLOPs)

Inference is one third of that per token: about $1.35 \times 10^{10}$ FLOPs per generated token, or 13 GFLOPs per token. On an H100 doing ~1 PFLOP/s of bf16, that's a theoretical floor of 13 microseconds per token — in practice, memory bandwidth dominates and you get more like 20–30 ms/token, but the FLOP floor sets the lower bound.

For training: an H100 cluster running at 40% model FLOPs utilization (a realistic large-scale number) delivers $0.4 \times 10^{15} = 4 \times 10^{14}$ effective FLOP/s per chip. To finish in a month (~$2.6 \times 10^6$ seconds), you need:

chips needed ≈ C / (FLOP/s per chip × seconds × utilization)
            ≈ 4.04e22 / (1e15 × 2.6e6 × 0.4)
            ≈ 39 chips

Forty H100s for a month gets you a from-scratch 7B-on-1T-tokens run. Llama-3-405B on 15T tokens, by the same math, is about $3.6 \times 10^{25}$ FLOPs — roughly 1000× more — which is why Meta used a 16,000-GPU cluster.

Why people sometimes say the answer is $6ND$ and sometimes $C \approx 6ND + \text{stuff}$

The 6ND formula counts the linear-layer matmuls. The "stuff" is everything else: attention's $QK^\top$ and $\text{softmax}\cdot V$ products (which depend on sequence length, not parameter count); the embedding and de-embedding (input lookup and output logit projection over vocabulary $V$); layer norms; activations; biases.

When Kaplan et al. wrote down their per-token tally, every term was there explicitly. The 6ND rule is what you get after dropping the small ones. Adam Casson's blog post breaks the components out explicitly — for GPT-3-scale models with sequence length 4096, the attention term is under 3% of total compute and the embedding/de-embedding are under 1%. Over the course of training, ignoring them puts your estimate within the bf16/fp32 mixed-precision noise.

Advanced When attention takes over, MoE, and reinforcement learning

Attention's quadratic term, and when it actually matters

Self-attention has two matrix multiplies whose inner dimension is the sequence length $T$, not a learned hidden size. For each layer, each head, each token in the sequence:

  • $QK^\top$: producing a $T \times T$ matrix of scores. Cost: $2 T d$ FLOPs per token (dot product of length $d_{\text{head}}$ across $T$ keys, summed over all heads).
  • $\text{softmax} \cdot V$: aggregating values weighted by attention. Cost: another $2 T d$ FLOPs per token.

Total attention FLOPs per token, summed over $L$ layers, are approximately $4 L T d$. Compare to the linear layers, $\approx 24 L d^2$. Attention overtakes the linear layers when:

$$4 L T d \;\gtrsim\; 24 L d^2 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad T \;\gtrsim\; 6 d$$

For Llama-2-7B with $d = 4096$, that crossover lives at about 24,000 tokens. For Llama-3-70B with $d = 8192$, it's near 50,000. With "1M context" frontier models, attention can become genuinely the dominant term — though by then almost everyone is using some attention sparsity (sliding windows, ring attention, sparse mixture-of-attention experts) precisely so this doesn't happen. The exact ratio is sensitive to the head structure (multi-query, grouped-query) which can shrink attention's coefficient by 4× or more.

For the 4k–32k context regime where most pretraining lives today, attention is typically 1–10% of compute. You can safely keep using 6ND.

Mixture-of-experts: $N$ becomes "active parameters"

Modern frontier models — DeepSeek-V3, Mixtral, GPT-4 (allegedly), Claude (rumoured) — use mixture-of-experts (MoE) feed-forward blocks. Instead of one $d \to 4d \to d$ MLP, the layer has $E$ such MLPs ("experts") plus a router that picks $k$ of them per token. The total parameter count is multiplied by $E$, but each token only flows through $k$ of the experts.

For FLOP accounting, what matters is active parameters $N_{\text{active}}$ — the parameters actually used per token, which is the dense parameter count plus $k/E$ of the expert parameters. The 6ND rule becomes $C \approx 6 N_{\text{active}} D$. DeepSeek-V3 has 671B total parameters but ~37B active, so its training cost is governed by 37B, not 671B.

This is exactly why MoE is attractive: you get the representational capacity of a 671B model at the FLOP cost of a 37B one (plus router overhead, which is small, and memory and bandwidth pressure, which is not). For inference, the $2N_{\text{active}}$ rule applies — but the model still occupies 671B of weights in memory, so you need much more high-bandwidth memory (HBM) per chip than a dense 37B would require.

Inference: just the forward half

Inference does no backward pass. The cost is $2N$ FLOPs per generated token (autoregressive decoding generates one token at a time, each token requiring one forward pass over the whole model). Reiner says this directly in the interview. The kicker: at inference time, FLOPs are usually not the bottleneck — memory bandwidth is. You spend most of your time loading the $N$ parameters from HBM into the chip, doing your $2N$ FLOPs of arithmetic with them, and dropping them. That asymmetry between training (compute-bound, the 6ND rule directly predicts wall-clock) and inference (bandwidth-bound, FLOPs are a lower bound but not the binding constraint) is why MatX, Cerebras, Groq, and others build chips with very different ratios of FLOPs to bandwidth.

Reinforcement learning: 2 to 6 ND

Reiner gives the formula "$2$ to $6 ND$" for reinforcement learning (RL). Where does the range come from?

An RL training step has two parts:

  1. Generation (rollout): the policy generates responses to prompts. This is just inference, so it costs $2N$ per generated token. If you generate $D_{\text{gen}}$ tokens, that's $2 N D_{\text{gen}}$ FLOPs.
  2. Training (gradient update): you take some subset of those rollouts (or all of them) and run a forward+backward pass to update the policy. That costs $6 N D_{\text{train}}$ FLOPs, where $D_{\text{train}}$ is the number of tokens you actually train on.

If you backpropagate through every generated token (e.g., REINFORCE-style, training on the entire trajectory), then $D_{\text{train}} = D_{\text{gen}}$ and the total is $2N D + 6N D = 8 N D$. That's the upper bound. In practice, people quote it loosely as "$6ND$" because the generation and training tokens largely overlap.

If you only backpropagate through a small fraction — for example, you generate long chains of thought but only update on the final reward signal, or you filter rollouts by reward and only train on the survivors — then $D_{\text{train}} \ll D_{\text{gen}}$ and the total approaches $2 N D_{\text{gen}}$. That's the lower bound.

There's also a machine-time inefficiency: rollout generation is bandwidth-bound (autoregressive decoding), so the chip spends most of its FLOPs idle while waiting for memory. Training is compute-bound. So the FLOPs are a mediocre proxy for wall-clock cost in RL, much worse than in pretraining. Reiner's "2 to 6" is right in the abstract; in practice, RL on an inference-tuned chip can cost less wall-clock and FLOPs than you'd expect, while RL on a training-tuned chip can spend most of its time waiting on rollouts.

Counting your own model

Three practical tools for verifying the formula on real code:

  • torch.profiler with FLOP counting: wraps a forward pass and reports per-operator FLOPs.
  • calflops: a popular pip-installable library that takes a model and a sample input shape and prints a per-layer FLOP breakdown, including handling of attention's quadratic term.
  • Manual count: for a transformer, compute $12 L d^2 \cdot D$ for the linear layers and add $4 L T d \cdot D$ for attention. Compare to the framework's number; they should agree to within a few percent.

One useful sanity-check unit is "model FLOPs utilization" (MFU), the ratio of (6ND ÷ training time) to (peak chip FLOP/s × number of chips). Numbers above 50% MFU are excellent; numbers below 30% suggest either communication overhead, poor kernel efficiency, or — sometimes — that your "6ND" estimate is missing terms that the actual hardware is computing (long context attention, recompute for activation checkpointing, etc.).

Activation checkpointing (also called gradient or activation recomputation) is worth a brief note. If you don't store all intermediate activations during the forward pass, you have to recompute some of them during the backward pass. A common scheme recomputes the entire forward, raising total cost from $6ND$ to $8ND$. The factor 6 is the lower bound assuming no recompute; "machine FLOPs" (what your hardware actually executes) often comes in higher.

Open questions and what to read next

  • Does 6ND survive ultra-long context training? Frontier labs are training on 1M+ token contexts now, where attention rivals or exceeds the linear layers. What's the right summary statistic — "$6ND + $ attention term", or a fully separate accounting?
  • How does sparsity (MoE, attention sparsity, structured pruning) interact with FLOP accounting? Active parameters work for MoE; what's the right convention for an irregular sparse attention pattern that varies per layer?
  • How well does FLOPs predict loss? Chinchilla's scaling laws say loss is a clean function of $C$ alone. But the same $C$ spent on a wide-shallow model versus a narrow-deep one gives different losses; the same $C$ at long context gives different loss than at short context. The 6ND abstraction hides architectural detail.
  • FP8, FP4, and the meaning of a "FLOP": when an H100 advertises 4 PFLOP/s in FP8, are those the same kind of FLOP as bf16 FLOPs? They're not. Lower-precision FLOPs require careful loss scaling, accumulator widening, and sometimes mixed-precision dance steps that the abstract FLOP count ignores.
  • RL FLOP accounting in practice: when DeepSeek-R1 reports its training compute, what fraction is in the SFT, the RL rollouts, and the policy updates? There's no standard answer yet.
