Roofline Analysis from First Principles

A back-of-the-envelope model that, in two lines of arithmetic, tells you whether your kernel is starving for bytes or starving for math.

performance modeling accelerators transformers
TL;DR

The roofline model says the runtime of any kernel is at least the larger of two quantities: the time to do its floating-point work at peak throughput, and the time to move its operands at peak bandwidth. Divide work by data movement to get arithmetic intensity in floating-point operations per byte (FLOP/byte). If your kernel's intensity is above the chip's ridge point, you are compute-bound; below it, you are memory-bound. On an H100 in bfloat16 the ridge point sits near 295 FLOP/byte, which is exactly why a transformer's weight-matrix multiplications need batch sizes in the hundreds before they stop being limited by high-bandwidth memory (HBM), and why the attention operation and layer normalization are essentially always memory-bound.

Why this matters

The Dwarkesh Patel blackboard interview with Reiner Pope opens with what Reiner calls the two principles of analysis: the time it takes to operate on the model's weights, and the time it takes to operate on the context (the key-value cache). He then writes a single inequality on the board: total time is at least the maximum of the memory time and the compute time. From that one line he derives almost everything else in the conversation, including why short prompts are cheap, why long-context decoding gets expensive, and why MatX exists at all.

That inequality is the roofline model. Once you internalize it, large language model (LLM) economics, accelerator design tradeoffs, and the entire flash-attention literature collapse into a single picture you can sketch on a napkin. This tutorial builds the picture from scratch, derives the numbers for an H100, and applies it to each piece of a transformer block. By the end you should be able to: draw a roofline plot for any chip given two spec-sheet numbers, place any kernel on it given a count of FLOPs and bytes, and explain in one sentence why decoding a single token from a 70-billion-parameter model is bandwidth-bound rather than compute-bound.

Beginner Two clocks running in parallel

The setup

Imagine a kernel — say, multiplying two matrices — running on an accelerator. To finish, two things must happen. First, the chip must perform some number of arithmetic operations $W$ (measured in floating-point operations, or FLOPs). Second, the chip must move some number of bytes $Q$ between the on-chip register file and the off-chip HBM. The chip can do both at once: while one number is being read from memory, the multiplier-accumulators (MACs) on the tensor cores can be grinding through earlier numbers. So the wall-clock time is bounded below by whichever of the two takes longer:

$$T \;\geq\; \max\!\left(\frac{W}{\pi},\;\frac{Q}{\beta}\right)$$

where $\pi$ is the chip's peak compute throughput in FLOP per second and $\beta$ is its peak memory bandwidth in bytes per second. That is the roofline inequality. Everything else in this tutorial is consequences of that one expression.

Arithmetic intensity

The natural thing to ask next is: which of the two terms is bigger? Define the kernel's arithmetic intensity (sometimes called operational intensity) as the ratio of work to data movement:

$$I \;=\; \frac{W}{Q} \quad \text{(FLOP per byte)}$$

Then the compute term is bigger exactly when $W/\pi > Q/\beta$, i.e. when $I > \pi/\beta$. The right-hand side is a property of the hardware alone — the ridge point — and the left-hand side is a property of the kernel alone. That separation is what makes the model so useful. You can compute $I$ once, on paper, and immediately know whether memory or compute is your enemy on any chip whose $\pi$ and $\beta$ you can look up.

A first numerical example

Consider the kernel $y = a \cdot x + b$ applied to a vector of $n$ bfloat16 (2-byte) elements. Each output requires one multiply and one add, so $W = 2n$ FLOPs. Each output requires reading one input element and writing one output element, so $Q = 4n$ bytes (ignore $a$ and $b$, which are tiny scalars). The intensity is

$$I \;=\; \frac{2n}{4n} \;=\; 0.5 \text{ FLOP/byte}.$$

That is well below any modern accelerator's ridge point, so the kernel is hopelessly memory-bound. No matter how fast the multiplier is, the chip will spend almost all its time waiting for HBM. Doubling the FLOPs (changing $a \cdot x + b$ to a degree-4 polynomial) does not change the runtime; halving the bytes (computing in 8-bit) does.

The H100 ridge point

For an NVIDIA H100 SXM5, the spec sheet gives a peak dense bfloat16 tensor-core throughput of roughly 989 teraFLOPs per second (TFLOPs/s) and a peak HBM3 bandwidth of about 3.35 terabytes per second (TB/s). Plugging in:

$$\frac{\pi}{\beta} \;=\; \frac{989 \times 10^{12} \text{ FLOP/s}}{3.35 \times 10^{12} \text{ B/s}} \;\approx\; 295 \text{ FLOP/byte}.$$

So on an H100 in bfloat16, any kernel with intensity below 295 is memory-bound and any kernel above is compute-bound. That number is the single most useful constant in modern ML systems work, and you will see it appear over and over.

What about the B200?

For NVIDIA's Blackwell B200, the dense bfloat16 throughput rises to about 2250 TFLOPs/s and HBM3e bandwidth to roughly 8 TB/s. The ratio is

$$\frac{2250}{8} \;\approx\; 281 \text{ FLOP/byte}.$$

Almost the same number. This is not a coincidence: across generations of NVIDIA, AMD, and Google chips, the ridge point in bfloat16 has stayed in the 200–400 range for a decade. Compute and bandwidth roughly co-scale, because the workload designers care about (matrix multiplications inside transformers) lives in that intensity range. Lower-precision regimes shift the picture: in FP8 the ridge point on an H100 is around 590 FLOP/byte, because the tensor cores get faster but HBM stays the same speed.

Intermediate Drawing the roof and reading the plot

The original paper

The picture, the name, and the analytical recipe come from Sam Williams, Andrew Waterman, and David Patterson's 2009 Communications of the ACM article "Roofline: An Insightful Visual Performance Model for Multicore Architectures" [1]. Their motivation was empirical: in 2008 multicore central processing units (CPUs) were diverging wildly in compute-to-bandwidth ratios, and developers needed a quick visual way to see whether any given kernel could plausibly hit peak. Their answer was a log-log plot whose x-axis is operational intensity in FLOP/byte and whose y-axis is achieved performance in FLOP/s. The maximum the hardware can deliver as a function of intensity is the upper envelope of two lines:

$$P(I) \;=\; \min(\pi,\;\beta \cdot I).$$

The right term, $\beta \cdot I$, is a diagonal line of slope 1 on log-log axes (the bandwidth-limited "slanted roof"). The left term, $\pi$, is a horizontal line (the compute-limited "flat roof"). Where they meet — at $I = \pi/\beta$ — is the ridge point. The whole picture looks like a roof, hence the name.

Reading the plot

To place a kernel on the plot, do three calculations: count its FLOPs $W$, count the bytes $Q$ it must move from HBM, and time how long it takes (or estimate from a profiler). Then plot the point $(W/Q,\, W/T)$. The vertical distance from your point to the roof is the speedup that is theoretically still on the table. If you sit right at the slanted roof you have saturated bandwidth and the only way up is to redesign the kernel to need fewer bytes (e.g. operator fusion). If you sit right at the flat roof you have saturated the tensor cores and the only way faster is more parallelism, lower precision, or sparsity. If you sit far below the roof, something else is going on — kernel launch overhead, synchronization, suboptimal cache use — and that is where profiler tools like NVIDIA Nsight or the Berkeley Empirical Roofline Toolkit (ERT) earn their keep.

The memory hierarchy

The single-roof picture is a simplification. Real chips have several layers of memory, each with its own bandwidth. From fastest and smallest to slowest and largest:

Each layer has its own bandwidth, so each layer has its own slanted roof. A modern roofline analysis often stacks them — the "hierarchical roofline" — to reveal which layer of the hierarchy is the actual bottleneck. A kernel that streams from HBM might be perfectly efficient at HBM bandwidth but still be far below the SRAM roof; a kernel that fits entirely in SRAM might be limited by L2 traffic instead. We will return to this when we talk about flash attention.

A worked numerical example: dense matmul on an H100

Take a square matrix multiplication $C = A B$ with $A, B, C$ all of shape $N \times N$ in bfloat16. The work is $W = 2 N^3$ FLOPs (one multiply and one add per inner-product term). The data movement, if every operand is fetched once from HBM, is $Q = 3 \cdot 2 N^2 = 6 N^2$ bytes. So intensity is

$$I \;=\; \frac{2N^3}{6N^2} \;=\; \frac{N}{3} \text{ FLOP/byte}.$$

For the H100 ridge point of 295, we need $N \gtrsim 885$ to be compute-bound. Anything smaller and the matmul is bandwidth-limited. (In practice, tiling lets you achieve much higher effective intensity by reusing tiles in SRAM, which is why even modestly-sized matmuls run near peak.) For $N = 4096$, intensity is $\approx 1365$ FLOP/byte, comfortably above the ridge — so the kernel runs at peak compute and finishes in $W/\pi = 2 \cdot 4096^3 / (989 \cdot 10^{12}) \approx 139$ microseconds (μs).

Why a transformer matmul looks like a tall-skinny matmul

Inside a transformer, the weight matmul has shape $Y = X W$ where $X$ is $B \times D$ (batch tokens by hidden dimension) and $W$ is $D \times D$. The work is $W = 2 B D^2$ and the bytes (assuming the weight matrix is the dominant cost, which it is when $B \ll D$) are $Q \approx 2 D^2$ for the weight plus $2 B D$ for activations. For $B$ small relative to $D$, intensity reduces to

$$I \;\approx\; \frac{2 B D^2}{2 D^2} \;=\; B \text{ FLOP/byte}.$$

A clean rule of thumb: the arithmetic intensity of a transformer's weight matmul is the batch size in tokens. To stay compute-bound on an H100 in bfloat16, $B$ must exceed roughly 295. This is the famous "critical batch size for inference" you will hear about constantly — and it is what Reiner means in the interview when he says you need to gather hundreds of tokens through batch size or speculative decoding before serving large language models becomes economical.

Advanced Transformers, hierarchical rooflines, and what the model misses

The three operations of a transformer block, on the same plot

A standard decoder block performs three kinds of work: (1) weight matrix multiplications (Q/K/V projections, attention output projection, and the multilayer-perceptron linear layers), (2) the attention operation itself (the $QK^\top$ and softmax-weighted $V$ products), and (3) elementwise + reduction ops (layer normalization, residual addition, activation functions). The roofline analysis for each is qualitatively different.

Weight matmuls

As we just derived, $I \approx B$ tokens. At training time, $B$ is comfortably in the thousands and matmuls are firmly compute-bound. At inference prefill (where you process the whole prompt at once), $B$ equals the prompt length, also typically large. At inference decode, where you generate one token at a time, $B$ might be 1 per request, and you only get into the compute-bound regime by batching many concurrent requests together — which is exactly what production serving systems like vLLM and TensorRT-LLM are built around.

Attention

The attention kernel is structurally different. For decode-time attention with batch size $B$, sequence length $S$, head dimension $d_h$, and $H$ query heads sharing $G$ key/value (KV) heads (multi-query / grouped-query attention), the work and bytes are roughly

$$W \approx 4 B H S d_h, \qquad Q \approx 2 B G S d_h \text{ (the KV cache)}.$$

So intensity is

$$I \;\approx\; \frac{4 B H S d_h}{2 B G S d_h} \;=\; \frac{2 H}{G}.$$

Three things jump out. First, the $S$ cancels — long context does not improve intensity. Second, the $B$ cancels — increasing batch size does not help either, because each request has its own KV cache. Third, intensity is set entirely by the architectural ratio $H/G$. For multi-head attention with $H = G$ this gives $I \approx 2$, hopelessly memory-bound. For grouped-query attention with $H/G = 8$, $I \approx 16$, still well below the ridge point. This is exactly Reiner's "principle two": the time to operate on the context is governed by the time to read the KV cache from HBM, not by tensor-core throughput.

Layer normalization, softmax, residuals

These are elementwise or near-elementwise operations. For layer norm over a $D$-dimensional vector you do roughly $5D$ FLOPs (mean, variance, normalize, scale, shift) and read/write $4D$ bytes in bfloat16, giving $I \approx 1$. Softmax is similar. Residual addition is even worse, with $I \approx 0.25$. All of these are deeply memory-bound — the only optimization that helps is fusing them into the surrounding matmul or attention kernel so their data never round-trips through HBM. This is why every production transformer kernel library you have ever heard of fuses everything it can.

The hierarchical roofline and flash attention

The basic single-roof model assumes "data movement" means HBM traffic. For a fused kernel that loads tiles into on-chip static random-access memory (SRAM) and does many FLOPs per byte loaded, the relevant denominator is HBM traffic, not the total arithmetic operating on intermediate values. Flash attention is the canonical example. Naive attention materializes the $S \times S$ score matrix in HBM, which forces $\Theta(S^2)$ bytes of traffic per layer and ruins the intensity for long sequences. Tri Dao's flash attention rewrites the kernel to tile $Q$, $K$, $V$ in SRAM and never write the score matrix back to HBM, replacing $O(S^2)$ HBM traffic with $O(S^2 d_h^2 / M)$ where $M$ is on-chip memory size. On the roofline picture, this moves attention upward and rightward — closer to the compute roof — without changing any FLOPs.

The lesson is general: when an operation appears stuck on the slanted roof, the right question is not "how do I compute faster?" but "can I rearrange the algorithm to keep more data in faster levels of the hierarchy?" Operator fusion, tiling, persistent kernels, and recomputation are all answers to that question.

Empirical rooflines

The numbers so far have been theoretical peaks. Actual chips fall short for many reasons, and Berkeley Lab's Empirical Roofline Toolkit (ERT) measures the achievable bandwidth and FLOP rates with carefully tuned microbenchmarks. The result is often a stack of "partial roofs" — separate ceilings for, say, fused-multiply-add vs. unfused FLOPs, or for streaming vs. cached HBM access patterns.

Limitations of the model

Roofline is a lower-bound on time, not a precise predictor. Several things can keep you below the roof even when arithmetic intensity says you should be at it:

  • Cache effects. If the working set does not fit in SRAM, you pay HBM traffic that the simple count of bytes does not capture.
  • Kernel launch overhead. A kernel that runs in microseconds is dominated by the few microseconds of launch latency. This is the "overhead-bound" third regime that Horace He highlights in Making Deep Learning Go Brrr [3].
  • Inter-device communication. Once you go multi-GPU, network bandwidth (NVLink, InfiniBand) introduces a third roof. In multi-host training the network roof is often lower than the HBM roof, and modeling it requires a more elaborate framework like the alpha-beta or LogP model.
  • Partial roofs. Tensor cores hit peak only with specific tile shapes, alignments, and operand layouts. Falling back to CUDA cores cuts effective $\pi$ by a factor of 30 or more.
  • Numerical precision. Spec sheets advertise sparse, FP4, or FP8 numbers that are unattainable for general workloads. Use the precision you actually run in.
  • Mixed-precision asymmetry. If your weights are int8 but your activations are bfloat16, the bytes-per-FLOP is no longer constant across the kernel, and the simple intensity formula needs to be averaged.

Despite these caveats, the model is astonishingly load-bearing in practice. When Reiner says he can sketch the economics of an entire LLM serving stack on a blackboard, he is doing roofline arithmetic — assigning each operation in the transformer to its dominant roof, summing the times, and reading off the cost.

Open questions and what to read next

  • How much of the gap between theoretical peak and achieved performance is fundamental versus engineering effort? Is there a principled "practical roofline" that accounts for instruction issue, register pressure, and warp scheduling?
  • How should the model be extended for sparse computation (mixture-of-experts, structured sparsity, Strassen-like algorithms)? See Reiner's blog post "Would Strassen matmuls be useful in AI if data movement were free?" [6].
  • What is the right roofline for distributed training, where the third axis is communication and the algorithms (AllReduce, AllGather, ReduceScatter) have their own intensity-like metric?
  • For non-LLM workloads (diffusion, recommenders, reinforcement learning) the kernel mix is different — does the ridge point still cluster operations as cleanly?
  • Can hardware design narrow the gap by raising bandwidth (HBM4, on-package DRAM) instead of compute, and what would the roofline look like for such a balanced chip? This is part of the case for specialized inference accelerators that MatX and others are building.
