NVLink, NVSwitch, and Rack Topology

A modern AI rack is one 72-GPU computer with copper down its spine. The physical reasons the next one isn't 5,000.

interconnect datacenter scale-up Blackwell
TL;DR

A modern AI training rack is organised around a scale-up domain: a tightly coupled set of GPUs sharing a fast all-to-all interconnect called NVLink, switched through chips called NVSwitches. NVIDIA's GB200 NVL72 puts 72 Blackwell GPUs along the outside of a rack and an NVSwitch fabric down the middle, so every GPU reaches every other in two hops at 1.8 TB/s. Step outside the rack and the scale-out network (InfiniBand or Ethernet) is roughly 8× slower per GPU. The constraints that cap how big a scale-up domain can be aren't logical; they're cabling density, bend radius, weight, power, and cooling. Hopper had 8 GPUs in a domain. Blackwell has 72. Rubin Ultra is aiming at 576, which needs optical interconnects and 600 kW racks. Once you have this geometry in your head, SemiAnalysis articles, NVIDIA roadmaps, and TPU pod papers read fluently.

Why this matters

When Reiner sketches an NVL72 on the blackboard, he's describing a 1.5-ton, 120 kW machine that lists for over three million dollars and behaves, from a software perspective, like one computer with 72 GPUs and roughly 13.5 TB of HBM. The fabric inside is fast enough that you can run model parallelism across all of it without paying much of a performance tax.

This rack is the unit of currency for modern AI. A "100,000 GPU cluster" is about 1,400 of these racks lined up in a warehouse. The scale-out network between racks is roughly 8× thinner per GPU than the network inside one. That asymmetry shapes almost everything downstream: how transformer training is sharded, how inference is batched, and where the next architectural ideas are likely to come from.

Once you understand the rack, you understand why tensor parallelism degree is usually 8 (Hopper) or up to 72 (Blackwell), why pipeline parallelism is a second-class citizen, why expert parallelism in MoE is so painful, and why the hyperscalers are in a knife fight over copper, optics, and switch silicon.

Beginner Wires, switches, and what a "GPU" really is

What "GPU" means in this context

When someone says "an H100 GPU" they usually mean an SXM5 module: a small printed circuit board, perhaps the size of a paperback, holding the GH100 silicon die, eighty gigabytes of High-Bandwidth Memory (HBM3), voltage regulators, and a connector on the back. The connector exposes three classes of wires:

  • Power and ground — about 700 watts at peak, which is why rack-scale machines are liquid cooled.
  • PCIe — the legacy path back to the host CPU, used for booting, storage, and modest data movement. Around 64 GB/s on a Gen5 ×16 link.
  • NVLink — the fast GPU-to-GPU lanes. On Hopper, 18 NVLink-4 ports each running at 50 GB/s bidirectional, totaling 900 GB/s per GPU.

NVLink is the only one that matters for our story. It is an NVIDIA-proprietary, point-to-point serial link, conceptually similar to PCIe but with a thinner protocol stack, custom physical layer, and an enormously higher per-pin data rate. On Blackwell (NVLink 5) every link runs at 100 GB/s bidirectional and there are still 18 links per GPU, totaling 1.8 TB/s per GPU. To put that in human terms: one Blackwell GPU can shuffle the entire contents of a typical laptop's RAM to a peer GPU about twice every second.

Scale-up vs. scale-out

The two-tier mental model is the most important idea on this page.

Scale-up means making a single tightly coupled "computer" larger by adding more GPUs that share one fast, all-to-all switch fabric. From software's perspective, a scale-up domain is the largest set of devices that you can treat as if they were all on one motherboard. Bandwidth between any pair is high, latency is low, and you can run collective operations like all-reduce or all-to-all without performance falling off a cliff.

Scale-out means lining up many such "computers" and connecting them through a hierarchical packet-switched network — typically NVIDIA InfiniBand or Ethernet over RoCE — so that you can train across thousands of GPUs. Per-GPU bandwidth here is roughly an order of magnitude lower, latency is microseconds rather than hundreds of nanoseconds, and the topology is a tree (leaf-spine or fat-tree) rather than a flat all-to-all.

The NVL72 rack has 1.8 TB/s per GPU on the scale-up side and 0.1 TB/s per GPU (one 800 Gb/s Network Interface Card) on the scale-out side. That ratio — roughly 18× — is the cliff Reiner is pointing at.

NVSwitch: the chip in the middle

If every GPU has 18 NVLinks, where do those links go? In a small system you could connect them directly to peer GPUs (a "mesh"). But a full mesh of N GPUs requires N(N-1)/2 links, which blows up fast. NVIDIA's solution since 2018 has been the NVSwitch: a dedicated packet switch chip whose only job is to route NVLink traffic between GPUs.

Architecturally, NVSwitch is to NVLink what an Ethernet switch is to Ethernet. Each port is an NVLink, and the switch's crossbar lets any port forward packets to any other port at full line rate. Crucially, NVSwitch supports SHARP (Scalable Hierarchical Aggregation and Reduction Protocol): the switch can perform reductions (sum, min, max) on packets as they pass through, accelerating all-reduce by avoiding the round-trip through GPU compute units. The fifth-generation NVSwitch ASIC used in GB200 NVL72 has 144 NVLink ports, each running at 100 GB/s bidirectional.

A worked example: Hopper HGX H100

Hopper's basic unit was the HGX H100 8-GPU baseboard — a single mainboard with eight H100 SXM5 modules and four 3rd-generation NVSwitch chips, all on one piece of PCB. Each H100 has 18 NVLinks of which all are wired to the four NVSwitches (so each NVSwitch gets 4 or 5 links from each GPU — divided by four GPU-to-switch lanes per direction).

Aggregate "all-to-all" intra-baseboard NVLink bandwidth: 8 GPUs × 900 GB/s ≈ 7.2 TB/s. Anywhere outside the baseboard, you exit the scale-up domain and enter scale-out InfiniBand or Ethernet. That is the world Reiner means when he says "Hopper had 8 in the scale-up domain."

Intermediate Inside the NVL72 rack

The leap from 8 to 72

For Blackwell, NVIDIA moved the NVSwitches off the baseboard and into separate switch trays that sit in the middle of the rack. This is the architectural move that unlocks 72-GPU scale-up. Each tray is no longer a self-contained 8-GPU island; it becomes a compute tray wired by passive copper cables (technically a direct-attach cable backplane) up to a switch fabric shared by the entire rack.

Concrete numbers

  • 72 Blackwell GPUs in 18 compute trays (4 GPUs and 2 Grace CPUs per 1U tray).
  • 9 NVSwitch trays with 2 NVSwitch5 ASICs each, for 18 ASICs total. Each ASIC has 144 NVLink ports at 100 GB/s, giving 14.4 TB/s of bidirectional switching capacity per chip.
  • Each GPU's 18 NVLinks are split across the 18 NVSwitch ASICs — exactly one link per GPU per ASIC. That is what makes it a flat, single-tier, two-hop fabric.
  • Aggregate NVLink bandwidth: 72 GPUs × 1.8 TB/s = 129.6 TB/s (often quoted as "130 TB/s").
  • Copper backplane: roughly 5,000 coaxial cables, ~3 km (≈ 2 miles) of copper, all blind-mate connectors at the rear of trays.
  • Power: ~120 kW per rack from 415 V three-phase AC bus bars (transitioning to 800 V DC for Rubin).
  • Liquid cooling: ~80% of waste heat removed by direct-to-chip cold plates; air handles the rest.
  • Scale-out NICs: one ConnectX-8 SuperNIC per GPU at 800 Gb/s (= 100 GB/s) for InfiniBand or RoCE Ethernet.

Worked example: scale-up versus scale-out bandwidth

This is the calculation Reiner wants you to be able to do in your head. Let's work it explicitly.

Inside one NVL72 rack (scale-up): 72 GPUs × 1.8 TB/s per-GPU NVLink = 129.6 TB/s aggregate. Bisection bandwidth — the minimum bandwidth crossing a "cut" that splits the GPUs in half — is half of that, ≈ 64.8 TB/s, because every cable enters and exits the cut once. (For an all-to-all switched fabric this is the right metric.) The two-hop diameter means the maximum latency between any pair of GPUs is about 1 µs.

Between racks (scale-out): each GPU has one 800 Gb/s NIC = 100 GB/s = 0.1 TB/s. So per rack: 72 × 0.1 TB/s = 7.2 TB/s of total scale-out injection bandwidth, and per-GPU scale-out bandwidth is exactly 18× lower than per-GPU scale-up bandwidth (1800 / 100 = 18). On a non-blocking fat-tree spine, the rack's bisection is also ~7.2 TB/s.

So inside the rack you have ~18× more bandwidth per GPU and ~9× more aggregate bisection than the connection to the next rack over. This is why Reiner says scale-out is "8× slower" — when you account for typical oversubscription in scale-out fat-trees and the fact that NVLink runs slightly above its raw line rate due to the absence of TCP-style protocol overhead, the practical ratio sits around 8–10× rather than the headline 18×.

What software does with this

The whole point of building a 72-GPU scale-up domain is that you can map the most bandwidth-hungry parallelism strategy — tensor parallelism (TP) — across all 72 GPUs without bottlenecking on the network. In tensor parallelism, every transformer layer's weight matrices are split across GPUs and each forward pass requires two all-reduces over the activations. Activation tensors at long context lengths can be tens of gigabytes, and you typically do dozens of TP all-reduces per microbatch; you simply cannot afford to push that traffic over scale-out.

On Hopper you were limited to TP=8. With NVL72 you can scale TP up to 72 — though in practice you split the rack: typically TP=8 for tensor parallelism plus expert parallelism (EP) up to 64 within the rack for mixture-of-experts models, or TP × pipeline-parallelism to fit 405B-parameter dense models in a single rack. Pipeline parallelism (PP) and data parallelism (DP), which are dominated by smaller and less frequent communications, can run over scale-out InfiniBand between racks.

SHARP and in-network reductions

NVSwitch implements NVIDIA SHARP at the hardware level. When 72 GPUs do an all-reduce, instead of every GPU sending its data to every other, the GPUs send partial sums up the switch fabric. The NVSwitches themselves perform the addition and broadcast the result back down. This roughly halves the on-the-wire bandwidth requirement and cuts latency by a factor of the topology's diameter. In practice it gives a 2–3× speedup on small all-reduce sizes, which dominate inference-time activations.

Advanced The physics of bigger domains, optics, and TPU pods

Why 72? The five physical constraints

In the interview Reiner is emphatic that the cap on scale-up size is physical, not protocol- or silicon-level. Five things conspire:

  1. Cable count. A flat, single-stage, all-to-all fabric of N GPUs each with 18 NVLinks requires 18N cable runs from compute to switch. At N=72 that's 1,296 NVLink runs, or roughly 5,000 individual coaxial pairs once you count each lane as a differential pair. Each pair is a fairly thick (3–5 mm) twin-ax cable. Bundle them and you get a tree-trunk-sized cable spine running the height of the rack.
  2. Bend radius. Twin-ax cables can't bend tighter than ~10× their diameter without wrecking signal integrity. So even if you can squeeze the cables in, you can't route them around corners gracefully. NVIDIA's engineering trick was the passive copper cable cartridge: pre-routed cable bundles that mate blind-attach to the rear of compute and switch trays, eliminating field-assembled connectors.
  3. Distance / signal integrity. Copper's reach at 200 Gb/s per pair (the NVLink-5 line rate) is roughly 1 m before reach extenders or retimers are needed. The NVL72 fits inside that envelope only because everything is co-located in one rack. Push to 144 GPUs in two racks and you immediately need optics or retimers, both of which cost watts and dollars.
  4. Weight and structural load. The NVL72 weighs about 1.4 metric tons. NVIDIA added over 100 lbs of steel just to handle the 6,000-lb mating force needed when sliding compute trays into the copper cartridge. Floor loading at the datacenter level becomes a real concern.
  5. Power and cooling. 120 kW in a single rack is roughly 4× higher than typical 2023-era cloud racks. Direct-to-chip liquid cooling becomes mandatory. The next-gen Rubin Ultra rack pushes 600 kW; that's a level where building new datacenters around the rack form factor (rather than retrofitting old ones) is unavoidable.

Notice the per-link rate doubles each generation (encoded as PAM4 modulation rather than NRZ from gen 4 onward), the link count plateaued at 18 in NVLink 4, and what changes most dramatically is the size of the scale-up domain. The numbers are getting silly: Rubin Ultra targets 576 GPU dies (144 dual-die packages × 4 dies-per-package or similar — NVIDIA's marketing math gets creative here) in a single 600 kW rack codenamed Kyber.

The optics transition

Every doubling of NVLink rate halves copper's reach. Beyond NVLink 6 (2026) it becomes very hard to do passive copper across rack scale, and NVIDIA is moving in two directions at once:

  • Co-Packaged Optics (CPO) on the scale-out side: silicon photonics engines integrated next to switch ASICs in the InfiniBand and Ethernet spine. Quantum-X Photonics and Spectrum-X Photonics are NVIDIA's product names. CPO eliminates the pluggable optical transceiver, saving roughly 5× the power per port. NVIDIA has explicitly said scale-out switches go optical first; scale-up domains will keep copper as long as physically possible because passive copper is much cheaper and more reliable.
  • Optical NVLink on the scale-up side: Rubin Ultra reportedly mixes copper for very local hops (within a sub-pod) and direct optical for cross-pod NVLink connections. The Kyber rack has compute blades rotated 90 degrees vertically into pods of 18 blades each, with NVLink switch blades inside the rack — and four such pods making up one 576-GPU domain.

The fundamental tradeoff: optical interconnects buy you reach (tens of meters instead of one meter) at the cost of ~10× the cost-per-bit and higher power per link end (the laser and photodiode burn watts). For dense scale-up, copper still wins until you genuinely cannot make it work. That is what Reiner means when he says cabling and bend radius are the constraints — they are the proximate barrier you hit before optics' economics get good enough to take over.

Bisection bandwidth as the costing metric

If you only remember one number from this tutorial, make it bisection bandwidth: cut the network in half, and measure the bandwidth crossing the cut. Bisection is what dominates the cost and performance of all-to-all collectives — the ones used in mixture-of-experts models, in expert parallelism, and in many forms of pipeline-rebalancing.

For an N-GPU all-to-all switched fabric where each GPU has bandwidth B to the switch, the bisection bandwidth is N×B/2 (every link crosses the cut twice in expectation, once per direction). For NVL72: 72 × 1.8/2 = 64.8 TB/s. For a 256-GPU scale-out domain where each GPU has 800 Gb/s (= 0.1 TB/s) and the spine is 1:1 non-blocking: 256 × 0.1 / 2 = 12.8 TB/s. Even though scale-out covers more GPUs, it has substantially less bisection per GPU. The cost per unit bisection is roughly the cost-per-port of switch silicon plus cabling — and switch silicon costs scale linearly with port count, while cable costs grow super-linearly because of the physical-density problems already discussed. This is why scale-up domains are the most expensive watts-per-square-meter you can buy in a datacenter.

Comparison: Google TPU pods

Google's TPU answers the same question with a completely different topology. Where NVIDIA builds flat all-to-all domains via packet-switched NVSwitch fabrics, Google uses 3D torus topologies wired with Optical Circuit Switches (OCS).

Concretely, a TPU v5p pod has 8,960 chips arranged in a 16×20×28 3D torus. Each chip has six inter-chip-interconnect (ICI) links, one to each face-neighbor. The pod's defining trick is the OCS layer: each rack's external links pass through MEMS-mirror optical switches that physically redirect light between fibers. The OCS lets Google: (a) carve the pod into job-sized "slices" of any 3D shape on demand, (b) route around failed chips and racks by reprogramming the mirrors, and (c) keep the logical topology a regular torus regardless of physical layout.

The torus uses a fixed 6-degree connectivity instead of NVIDIA's all-to-all switch fabric. That means TPU's per-chip bisection bandwidth is lower than a Blackwell GPU's, and the diameter is 3∛N hops instead of 2. But TPU compensates by tuning XLA's compiler to schedule communication as ring-based collectives that exploit the torus structure exactly — and by amortizing the OCS reconfiguration across many concurrent jobs.

This is the philosophical split Reiner references: NVIDIA bets on overwhelming bandwidth in a small flat domain that the application doesn't need to know about; Google bets on a smaller per-link bandwidth in a much larger structured domain whose topology the compiler exploits explicitly. Both approaches push toward pod-scale supercomputers, just by different routes.

The Rubin Ultra picture

Rubin Ultra (NVL576, late 2027) is what NVIDIA's slide deck looks like when you extrapolate every trend to its limit. Numbers worth knowing:

  • 576 GPU dies per rack (NVIDIA counts dies, not packages, when it says "576" — each Rubin Ultra package contains 4 reticle-stitched compute tiles, so 144 packages × 4 dies).
  • 600 kW per rack. This is so far past current datacenter norms that NVIDIA is shipping the Kyber rack with a custom 800 V DC busbar to reduce copper mass in the power delivery itself.
  • Two-level NVLink topology: full all-to-all within a 144-GPU "pod" (one of four pods per rack) using copper, then a higher-tier optical NVLink layer connecting pods within and across racks. This is the first time NVIDIA has admitted that flat scale-up does not scale.
  • NVLink 6 at 3.6 TB/s per GPU, double the Blackwell rate.
  • Direct optical NVLink: laser-on-package optics at the GPU edge, eliminating pluggable transceivers.

The interesting question — and one Reiner gets at in the interview — is whether 576 is a real number or a marketing one. Bend radius, weight, and cooling have not gotten easier. NVIDIA's solution is to abandon a single flat domain and adopt a two-tier hierarchy that Google has been shipping since 2018. Whether that hierarchy adds enough complexity to slow training (relative to a hypothetical truly-flat 576-way domain) is a question that will be answered empirically over the next two years.

Reading SemiAnalysis fluently

Once you have the rack model in your head, articles that previously read as gibberish become clear. Some recurring jargon, decoded:

  • "Backend network" = scale-out InfiniBand/Ethernet between racks, used for data parallel all-reduce and pipeline parallel sends.
  • "Frontend network" = ordinary Ethernet for storage, control plane, and operator access. Slow and uninteresting.
  • "Rail-optimized" = the scale-out fabric is built so that GPUs in the same "rail position" within their racks (e.g., all the GPU 0s) connect to the same leaf switch, optimizing the all-reduce path.
  • "NVL72 pod" / "SU" = "scalable unit," typically a small group of NVL72 racks (4–32) sharing a leaf-spine InfiniBand switch group.
  • "Optical transceivers per GPU ratio" = a proxy for how expensive scale-out is; NVL72 has 1:1 (one 800G transceiver per GPU at the GPU side, plus matching transceivers in the spine).
  • "Bisection oversubscription" = ratio of theoretical full bisection to actual provisioned. 1:1 is non-blocking; 2:1 saves cost but halves all-to-all bandwidth.

Open questions and what to read next

  • Is there a fundamental ceiling on copper-based scale-up? Most engineers think NVL72 is roughly 1.5× away from the limit; Rubin Ultra's hierarchical design implies NVIDIA agrees.
  • Will optical NVLink ever match copper's cost-per-bit at one-meter distances? Co-packaged optics have to clear ~5 pJ/bit to be competitive; current production sits around 15 pJ/bit.
  • Could a different topology (Dragonfly+, HammingMesh, slimfly) beat the fat-tree-plus-flat-spine approach? Academic results say yes; deployment inertia says no.
  • How does the rack form factor co-evolve with model architecture? The rise of MoE has dramatically increased all-to-all traffic, which is exactly the workload that benefits most from scale-up depth.
  • What is the right unit for purchasing? Right now the rack is, but training runs are starting to need multi-rack "super-pods" with consistent topology guarantees.
