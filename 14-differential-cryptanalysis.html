Differential Cryptanalysis (and Its Surprising Parallels to Gradient Descent)

How Biham and Shamir's 1990 attack on DES is structurally the same algorithm as adversarial-example generation in a deep network — and why Reiner Pope thinks ciphers and neural nets are siblings.

cryptanalysis block ciphers adversarial examples avalanche S-box
TL;DR

A block cipher and a deep neural network are both built from stacks of linear—then—nonlinear layers whose job is to mix information so thoroughly that no shortcut from input to output exists. Differential cryptanalysis, discovered publicly by Biham and Shamir in 1990 (and quietly known to IBM since 1974), attacks a cipher by tracking how a small input difference propagates through the rounds — literally a finite-difference derivative of the cipher. The same idea, run on a neural network with infinitesimal differences and called the gradient, is what we use to train nets and to break them with adversarial examples. The Fast Gradient Sign Method is differential cryptanalysis with a continuous activation function. After this page, you should be able to read Biham & Shamir's original paper, build the difference distribution table for a 4-bit S-box by hand, and explain to a machine-learning friend exactly why a cipher round and a Transformer block rhyme.

Why this matters

In Reiner Pope's blackboard interview with Dwarkesh, he keeps coming back to a thesis he laid out more carefully in his blog post Neural Net Ciphers: that modern neural networks and modern block ciphers look the way they do because of three forces — weak correctness requirements (only invertibility for a cipher, only differentiability for a net), a relentless need to mix every input bit into every output bit, and the economic pressure of running on real hardware. The two fields converged on the same primitive: a stack of identical rounds, each one a cheap linear transform followed by a cheap nonlinear transform, repeated until the state is thoroughly scrambled.

If the architectures rhyme, the attacks should rhyme too. They do. The most important attack on block ciphers in the last forty years — differential cryptanalysis — works by feeding two inputs that differ in one bit, watching how the difference grows as it passes through rounds, and using the round-by-round statistics to peel off the key. That is exactly what a researcher does to a neural network when they want to find an adversarial example: feed two inputs that differ in one pixel, follow the gradient, see which tiny perturbation flips the output the most. The gradient is the differential characteristic. Once you see this, several things click: why hash functions and ciphers want avalanche but Transformers' residual streams do not; why Goodfellow's Fast Gradient Sign Method works at all; and why a hardware company aiming at training and inference economics is the same kind of company that ought to design good cryptographic primitives.

This page is the pre-quel to tutorial 15 on the Feistel construction. By the end of this one you should be able to (a) read Biham and Shamir's 1990 paper at the level of working through their DES attack on paper, (b) build the difference distribution table of any small S-box, and (c) explain to a deep-learning audience why an adversarial example is a 1-round differential trail evaluated at infinite precision.

Beginner Block ciphers, mixing, and the avalanche effect

What a block cipher is

A block cipher is a function $E_K : \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}^n$ — it eats $n$ bits of plaintext, takes a key $K$, and produces $n$ bits of ciphertext. For each fixed $K$ it must be a bijection (otherwise you couldn't decrypt), and given only the ciphertext it must look indistinguishable from a random permutation to anyone who doesn't know $K$. The Data Encryption Standard (DES, 1977) used $n=64$ and a 56-bit key. The Advanced Encryption Standard (AES, 2001) uses $n=128$ and key sizes of 128, 192, or 256 bits.

Building a pseudorandom permutation directly from a key is hard, so practical ciphers stack many simple, identical rounds. AES-128 has 10 rounds. DES has 16. Each round mixes a little; together they mix a lot. The dominant pattern is the substitution-permutation network (SPN):

  1. Key mixing. XOR the round key into the state.
  2. Substitution. Pass each small chunk of the state through an S-box, a fixed nonlinear lookup table from $b$ bits to $b$ bits (typically $b=4$ or $b=8$).
  3. Permutation. A linear map — for example, a bit shuffle — that scatters the S-box outputs across the next round's S-box inputs.

The avalanche effect

The whole point of stacking rounds is the avalanche effect, a term coined by Horst Feistel in 1973: a one-bit change in the input should flip about half the output bits. Webster and Tavares formalised this in 1985 as the Strict Avalanche Criterion (SAC): every output bit should flip with probability $1/2$ when any single input bit is flipped. SAC is the cipher-design version of "the function should look random." If a single input bit only ever affected one output bit, an attacker could test it independently; avalanche prevents that.

Avalanche is achieved by composition. One pass through a 4-bit S-box can change up to 4 output bits. The P-box then sends those 4 changed bits into 4 different S-boxes in the next round, where each of them can each flip up to 4 more bits, and so on. After $\log_b n$ rounds — a handful for typical $n$ — every input bit can in principle reach every output bit. The actual avalanche kicks in a bit later because not every S-box input flip propagates with probability 1; differential cryptanalysis lives precisely in the gap between "can reach" and "does reach with probability 1."

Why mixing is the central design goal

If even one output bit depends weakly on the key, you can set up an experiment to learn that key bit, and then the rest follows. So a cipher designer wants every output bit to depend on every key bit and every plaintext bit, in a way that has no compact algebraic description. Linear maps cannot do this on their own — a linear function of the key composed with a linear function of the plaintext is still linear and thus easy to invert. The S-box is therefore where security is born; the rest of the round just spreads the nonlinearity around.

Now read Reiner's argument Neural Net Ciphers with this in your head and notice the symmetry. He writes that both ciphers and neural nets need "every output bit to depend on every input bit in complicated ways" and that both reward designs "where every part of the state interacts with every other part." A Transformer block is morally a key-mixing-substitution-permutation round: the residual stream is the state, attention is the cross-position permutation, the feed-forward sublayer is a per-position nonlinearity (the analog of the S-box), and the layer-norm scale parameters play a faint role of mixing-key. That analogy is not a coincidence; it is the consequence of two design problems with the same solution.

Intermediate A 4-bit S-box, a difference distribution table, and one round of attack

An S-box as a lookup table

Let's pick a concrete 4-bit S-box, the one Howard Heys uses in his standard tutorial (originally a row from a DES S-box). It maps a 4-bit input $x \in \{0,\dots,15\}$ to a 4-bit output $S(x)$:

$x$0123456789ABCDEF
$S(x)$E4D12FB83A6C5907

Sixteen entries, a permutation of the 16 possible 4-bit values. This is the cipher's only source of nonlinearity, and it is small enough to analyse by hand. Reading row by row: $S(0)=E$, $S(1)=4$, $S(2)=D$, and so on. There is no obvious algebraic pattern — that is the point.

The key idea: track differences, not values

Differential cryptanalysis pivots on a clever change of perspective. Instead of asking "given input $x$, what is $S(x)$?" we ask: given a pair of inputs $(x, x')$ with XOR-difference $\Delta x = x \oplus x'$, what is the distribution of the output difference $\Delta y = S(x) \oplus S(x')$?

If $S$ were linear over GF(2), then $S(x) \oplus S(x') = S(x \oplus x')$ and the output difference would be a deterministic function of the input difference — one input difference, one output difference, no key, no work. Because $S$ is nonlinear, the output difference is a random variable over the choice of $x$ (with $x'$ pinned to $x \oplus \Delta x$). The whole game is to find input differences whose output-difference distribution is far from uniform.

Building the difference distribution table

For a 4-bit S-box there are $16 \times 16 = 256$ pairs $(\Delta x, \Delta y)$. The difference distribution table (DDT) records, for each pair, how many of the 16 possible inputs $x$ produce that pair. Diagonal entry $(\Delta x, \Delta y) = (0,0)$ is always 16 (zero in, zero out, trivially). Every other row sums to 16 and contains only even numbers, because if $x$ achieves $\Delta y$ then so does $x' = x \oplus \Delta x$.

Concretely, to fill in the entry for $(\Delta x = B, \Delta y = 2)$: enumerate $x = 0, 1, \dots, F$, compute $x' = x \oplus B$, look up $S(x)$ and $S(x')$, check whether $S(x) \oplus S(x') = 2$, count the hits. The result for the Heys S-box is 8, meaning the differential $B \to 2$ (in hex) holds with probability $8/16 = 1/2$. Most other entries are 0, 2, or 4. The DDT for this S-box has a maximum non-trivial entry of 8 out of 16; a perfectly designed S-box (for any $b > 2$) cannot achieve a max smaller than 4, the so-called almost perfect nonlinear bound.

Worked example: one round of an attack

Imagine the simplest possible cipher: one S-box preceded by a XOR with a 4-bit key $K$. The ciphertext is $C = S(P \oplus K)$. We want $K$. Brute force takes 16 guesses. Differential cryptanalysis takes about 8.

  1. Choose a useful input difference. Pick $\Delta x = B$ because we know $\Delta x = B \to \Delta y = 2$ holds with probability 1/2.
  2. Query the encryption oracle. Pick a random plaintext $P$, also encrypt $P' = P \oplus B$. Internally, the cipher computes $S(P \oplus K)$ and $S(P' \oplus K) = S(P \oplus K \oplus B)$. So the inputs to $S$ differ by exactly $B$.
  3. Filter on output difference. Receive $C, C'$. Compute $\Delta y = C \oplus C'$. Half the time $\Delta y = 2$; discard the others.
  4. Recover the key. When $\Delta y = 2$, you know that the actual input pair to $S$ is one of the eight pairs $(u, u \oplus B)$ such that $S(u) \oplus S(u \oplus B) = 2$. Each candidate $u$ gives a candidate key $K = P \oplus u$. Repeat with another plaintext: only the correct $K$ will be consistent across all queries.

You have just done differential cryptanalysis. Two queries are usually enough at probability 1/2; in real ciphers the per-round probability is much smaller (often $2^{-3}$ to $2^{-7}$ per active S-box) and you need to chain rounds.

Linear cryptanalysis — the dual

Mitsuru Matsui's linear cryptanalysis (1993) is the perfect dual. Instead of asking what input/output difference holds with high probability?, he asked what linear combination of input, output, and key bits is biased away from probability 1/2? If you can find a linear approximation $a \cdot P \oplus b \cdot C \oplus c \cdot K = 0$ that holds with probability $1/2 + \epsilon$ for nonzero bias $\epsilon$, you can collect $O(\epsilon^{-2})$ known plaintexts and recover $c \cdot K$ by majority vote. Matsui used this to break DES with $2^{43}$ known plaintexts — better than brute force. The two attacks are siblings; modern ciphers like AES are designed to resist both.

The IBM history

One of the great stories in cryptography. When DES was published in 1977 its S-box design criteria were classified. The cryptographic community spent more than a decade staring at the eight DES S-boxes wondering if they hid a back door. Biham and Shamir's 1990 paper Differential Cryptanalysis of DES-like Cryptosystems reinvented the technique in public and noticed something strange: when applied to DES it barely worked, requiring $2^{47}$ chosen plaintexts. But when they tweaked the S-boxes — even tiny changes — DES became dramatically weaker.

In 1994 Don Coppersmith, one of DES's IBM designers, was permitted to publish what really happened. The IBM team had discovered the same attack in 1974, called it the T-attack (Tickle attack), and tuned the S-boxes specifically to resist it. Coppersmith wrote: "After discussions with NSA, it was decided that disclosure of the design considerations would reveal the technique of differential cryptanalysis, a powerful technique that could be used against many ciphers." The S-boxes were chosen so that no single differential characteristic propagates with usable probability through 16 rounds of DES. The classification looks paranoid in retrospect, but it cost the public-key community 16 years of effort to catch up.

Advanced Multi-round trails, key recovery, and the neural-net analogy

Differential characteristics across rounds

A real cipher has many rounds. A differential characteristic for an $r$-round cipher is a sequence $(\Delta_0, \Delta_1, \dots, \Delta_r)$ of intermediate differences. The probability of the whole trail is, under the standard independent rounds assumption, the product of per-round probabilities:

$$ \Pr[\Delta_0 \to \Delta_r] \approx \prod_{i=1}^{r} \Pr[\Delta_{i-1} \to \Delta_i \mid \text{round } i]. $$

Each per-round probability factors further into the product of probabilities at each active S-box (an S-box whose input difference is nonzero). An S-box with input difference 0 always outputs difference 0 with probability 1, so it is passive and contributes 1. The cryptanalyst's job is to find a trail with as few active S-boxes as possible and the highest possible per-S-box probability. The cipher designer's job is to ensure that every trail through $r$ rounds activates many S-boxes — this is what AES's "wide trail strategy" guarantees.

Key recovery: the last-round trick

Biham & Shamir's actual attack on $r$-round DES is more subtle than a direct $r$-round trail. They use an $(r-1)$-round trail that ends just before the last round. Then for each guess of the last-round key, they decrypt the final round and check whether the predicted $(r-1)$-round difference appears with the predicted bias. The correct guess produces the bias; wrong guesses average out to uniform.

Concretely, attack DES (16 rounds) with a 13-round characteristic of probability $p$. Collect $N \gg 1/p$ chosen plaintext pairs with input difference $\Delta_0$. For each candidate last-round subkey, peel off rounds 16, 15, 14 and count how often you see the predicted internal difference at round 13's output. The correct subkey wins. This reduces a 56-bit key search to a sequence of 6-bit subkey searches.

Now the punchline: gradient descent is differential cryptanalysis at infinitesimal scale

Hold the cipher round in your head: linear (XOR with key, P-box) then nonlinear (S-box). Hold a Transformer block next to it: linear (attention's QKV projections, MLP's input projection) then nonlinear (softmax, ReLU/GeLU). Same shape.

Now hold differential cryptanalysis in your head. We pick an input pair $(x, x')$ with small XOR difference, push it through the network, and ask which output difference is most likely. Replace XOR by subtraction, replace "small difference" by an infinitesimal $\epsilon$, replace "look up in DDT" by "linearise". You have just defined the gradient.

$$ \nabla_x f(x) = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \frac{f(x + \epsilon) - f(x)}{\epsilon}. $$

The chain rule that backpropagation uses to compose gradients across layers is the differential trail. The "trail probability" (product of per-S-box probabilities) becomes the product of Jacobians (the chain rule); the active S-box becomes a unit with nonzero local gradient.

Adversarial examples are 1-round attacks at infinite precision

Goodfellow et al. 2014 proposed the Fast Gradient Sign Method: given a classifier $f$ and an input $x$ with true label $y$, build the adversarial perturbation

$$ x_{\text{adv}} = x + \epsilon \cdot \mathrm{sign}(\nabla_x \mathcal{L}(f(x), y)). $$

Read this as: pick the input difference $\Delta x = \epsilon \cdot \mathrm{sign}(\nabla_x \mathcal{L})$ that maximally increases the output difference $\Delta y$ in the direction of misclassification. That is exactly what step 1 of differential cryptanalysis does: choose the input difference that maximises the probability of a useful output difference. FGSM is a 1-step differential characteristic of an $L$-layer neural network, evaluated at a specific input by linearising via the gradient. PGD (projected gradient descent attacks) are the multi-step version, the analog of multi-round trails.

Goodfellow's "linear hypothesis" makes the analogy almost embarrassing. He argued that adversarial examples exist because deep networks behave linearly enough at small scales that a high-dimensional perturbation aligned with the gradient produces a large output change. Cipher designers spend decades trying to ensure that their constructions have no such linearity at small scales — a high difference distribution table entry is a 4-bit version of an adversarial direction. Modern S-boxes are chosen so the maximum DDT entry is bounded; modern neural networks have no such guarantee, which is why adversarial robustness is so hard.

Where the analogy breaks: residual streams want non-avalanche

The analogy is not total. Cipher designers want avalanche everywhere: every output bit should depend on every input bit. Neural network designers want avalanche in some places (the parts that compute high-level concepts, attention sinks, or representations to be used downstream) and want the opposite — stability of the residual stream — in others.

A modern Transformer's residual stream is, by design, a slow-moving channel: each block adds a small update to a large running sum, and the contributions of any one layer are typically a small fraction of the norm. This is the opposite of avalanche. If a one-token change at the input flipped half the residual-stream coordinates by layer 5, training would be unstable and gradients would explode. The same Transformer is, however, expected to apply something like avalanche when computing a hash-like function (e.g. cryptographic-looking lookups in induction heads, or content-addressable memory in MLPs). So a Transformer is a partial cipher: avalanche in some sublayers, anti-avalanche along the residual.

This duality also explains why neural networks for cryptographic tasks (hash preimage finding, LWE, learning ciphers from examples) are conspicuously hard to train: the loss landscape at large input differences is the difference distribution table of the cipher, which has been engineered to be flat. There is no gradient signal to follow because the cipher designer made sure of it.

Hardware perspective

Reiner has spent his career thinking about how to make matrix multiplies and nonlinearities efficient. The same hardware argument explains both the SPN structure and the Transformer block: a wide, regular linear operation (matrix multiply or P-box) is bandwidth-limited and SIMD-friendly; a small per-element nonlinearity (S-box or activation) fits in a tiny lookup table or a few cycles of arithmetic; both compose into something deeper by repetition. AES's hardware throughput (gigabytes per second on a modern CPU's AES-NI instructions) and a transformer's hardware throughput (teraflops on a modern GPU) are governed by the same logic: keep the linear part near peak FLOPs, keep the nonlinear part cheap, and make the round shallow enough to pipeline.

Open questions and what to read next

  • Is there a "wide trail" theorem for Transformers? AES's wide trail strategy proves a lower bound on the number of active S-boxes per round. Is there an analogous lower bound on how many MLP neurons must "activate" to push an adversarial direction through? Robustness research is moving in this direction but no clean theorem yet exists.
  • Are gradient hackers a real failure mode? If the residual stream wants stability, but parts of the network want avalanche, an adversary who can choose inputs might find adversarial directions that bypass the stable channel. This is essentially what jailbreaks are doing.
  • Can S-box design ideas inform activation function design? S-boxes are chosen to minimise differential and linear bias. Could a "differentially private" or "low max-DDT" activation function reduce adversarial vulnerability? Some recent work (smooth nonlinearities, lipschitz-bounded layers) explores this.
  • Neural-network-as-cipher: what would training even mean? If a cipher-shaped network is supposed to resist gradient signal, can we still train it? Recent neural cryptanalysis papers (Gohr 2019, Bao et al.) train neural distinguishers against reduced-round ciphers and find them effective, suggesting the gap between the two design objectives is smaller than it looks.
  • Quantum implications. Grover's algorithm halves the effective key length but does not give a quantum analog of differential cryptanalysis. Could a quantum "differential" attack outperform the classical chosen-plaintext bound? Open as of 2026.
