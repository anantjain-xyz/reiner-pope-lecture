Flash Attention: v1 to v2 to v3

An IO-aware kernel that walked attention up the roofline plot, one GPU generation at a time.

attention GPU kernels IO-awareness tiling online softmax
TL;DR

Textbook attention materializes the $N \times N$ score and probability matrices in HBM, then reads them back to multiply by $V$ and again for the backward pass. The kernel is memory-bound, with quadratic HBM traffic. Flash Attention keeps those intermediates in on-chip SRAM by tiling the sequence dimension and running an online softmax recurrence. v1 (Dao et al., 2022) introduced the algorithm and the recomputation backward pass. v2 (Dao, 2023) reordered the loops and parallelized over sequence length to fill more of an A100. v3 (Shah et al., 2024) rebuilt the kernel around Hopper's asynchronous warp-group matmul (WGMMA), the tensor memory accelerator (TMA), and FP8, lifting H100 utilization from roughly 35% to 75% of peak.

Why this matters

While walking through prefill, Dwarkesh asks about the memory cost of attention "if you're doing flash attention," and Reiner waves it away: "It's basically temporary. It doesn't even go to main memory. Just ignore that." The lecture moves on. That one-line dismissal is the entire reason Flash Attention exists. In a textbook implementation, the score matrix $S = QK^\top$ and its row-softmax $P = \mathrm{softmax}(S)$ are full $N \times N$ tensors that get written to HBM and read back twice. For $N = 32{,}768$ tokens that's more than a gigabyte per head per layer. Arithmetic intensity craters. The matmul units sit idle waiting on the memory bus.

Flash Attention rearranges the kernel so $S$ and $P$ never leave the streaming multiprocessor. Tiles of $Q$, $K$, $V$ stream into SRAM, the partial softmax updates incrementally, and only the final output $O$ plus a small log-sum-exp vector touch HBM. Prefill and training attention move from memory-bound to compute-bound. The tensor cores are actually fed. Attention stops being the bottleneck of a long-context model and turns into a rounding error.

The three versions are also a tour of how kernels co-evolve with hardware. v1 fits Ampere. v2 extracts more parallelism from the same chip. v3 only makes sense once you have Hopper's asynchronous tensor cores and TMA. After reading this page you should be able to follow all three papers, point at where each speedup comes from, and explain to a colleague why "the kernel got 2× faster with no algorithmic change" is a perfectly reasonable sentence.

Beginner Why standard attention is slow

The textbook algorithm

Self-attention takes three matrices, all of shape $(N, d)$ where $N$ is the sequence length and $d$ is the head dimension (typically 64 or 128):

  • $Q$, the queries — what each token is looking for.
  • $K$, the keys — what each token is.
  • $V$, the values — what each token contributes if attended to.

The output $O$ is also $(N, d)$ and computed as

$S = QK^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}, \quad P = \mathrm{softmax}(S) \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}, \quad O = PV \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}.$

The softmax is row-wise: each row $S_i$ becomes $P_{ij} = e^{S_{ij} - m_i} / \sum_k e^{S_{ik} - m_i}$ where $m_i = \max_j S_{ij}$ is subtracted for numerical stability. So far so familiar.

The memory hierarchy

To see why this is slow, you have to look at the GPU's memory hierarchy. An NVIDIA A100 has roughly 40 MB of L2 cache, about 192 KB of SRAM (called shared memory) per streaming multiplier, and 40 or 80 GB of HBM. The bandwidth gap between SRAM and HBM is enormous: SRAM is on the order of 19 TB/s, HBM is around 1.5–2 TB/s. Anything you can keep in SRAM costs ten times less in time than anything you have to pull from HBM.

Counting the bytes

Now imagine running standard attention with $N = 8192$ and $d = 128$ in float16 (2 bytes per element). The $Q$, $K$, $V$ matrices are each $8192 \cdot 128 \cdot 2 = 2$ MB. Fine. But $S$ and $P$ are $8192^2 \cdot 2 = 128$ MB each. A naive implementation does this:

  1. Read $Q$ and $K$ from HBM, compute $S = QK^\top$, write $S$ to HBM. Traffic: $\approx 2(2 + 2 + 128) = 264$ MB.
  2. Read $S$ from HBM, compute $P$, write $P$ to HBM. Traffic: $\approx 256$ MB.
  3. Read $P$ and $V$ from HBM, compute $O = PV$, write $O$. Traffic: $\approx 132$ MB.

About 650 MB of HBM traffic to do roughly $4N^2 d = 34$ GFLOP of useful arithmetic. At A100's ~1.5 TB/s HBM bandwidth and 312 TFLOP/s tensor-core peak, the kernel is bottlenecked on memory by a factor of about ten. The tensor cores sit mostly idle. Doubling $N$ quadruples the HBM bill while compute only quadruples too, so the kernel never escapes its memory-bound regime.

The IO complexity of standard attention is $\Omega(Nd + N^2)$ HBM accesses. Flash Attention, as we will see, achieves $O(N^2 d^2 / M)$ where $M$ is the SRAM size. With $d = 128$ and $M \approx 100$ KB, that is roughly $9\times$ less HBM traffic. Dao et al. prove this is asymptotically optimal for any algorithm computing exact attention.

Why softmax is the hard part

If attention were just $QK^\top V$ — two matmuls in a row — you could fuse them by tiling. The problem is the softmax in the middle. Softmax is global: each row's normalizer $\ell_i = \sum_j e^{S_{ij} - m_i}$ depends on every entry in that row. You seem to need the entire row of $S$ before you can normalize. This is what forces standard attention to materialize $S$.

Or does it? The trick is that you do not need the whole row at once. You can update a running max and a running normalizer as more entries arrive, the way you can compute a streaming variance. That recurrence is the heart of Flash Attention. We will derive it next.

Intermediate Online softmax and the v1 kernel

Online softmax (Milakov and Gimelshein, 2018)

Suppose you have a vector $x = (x_1, \dots, x_N)$ and you want $\mathrm{softmax}(x)_i = e^{x_i - m} / \sum_j e^{x_j - m}$ where $m = \max_j x_j$. The naive algorithm makes three passes: one to find $m$, one to compute the unnormalized exponentials and their sum, and one to divide. Milakov and Gimelshein observed that you can fuse the first two passes by tracking a running max and a running normalizer as you stream through $x$, then doing a single division pass at the end.

Let $m^{(j)}$ and $\ell^{(j)}$ denote the running max and running normalizer after seeing the first $j$ elements. Initialize $m^{(0)} = -\infty$, $\ell^{(0)} = 0$. The recurrence is:

$m^{(j)} = \max\!\bigl(m^{(j-1)}, x_j\bigr),$

$\ell^{(j)} = \ell^{(j-1)} \cdot e^{m^{(j-1)} - m^{(j)}} + e^{x_j - m^{(j)}}.$

The key move is the rescaling factor $e^{m^{(j-1)} - m^{(j)}}$. Whenever a new element $x_j$ raises the running max, every previously accumulated exponential was computed with the wrong shift; multiplying the old normalizer by $e^{m^{(j-1)} - m^{(j)}}$ corrects all of them at once. If $m^{(j)} = m^{(j-1)}$ (no new max) the factor is 1 and nothing changes. After all $N$ elements, $m^{(N)}$ and $\ell^{(N)}$ equal the true max and normalizer.

This combiner generalizes to merging two arbitrary partial states $(m_1, \ell_1)$ and $(m_2, \ell_2)$ for disjoint subsets:

$m = \max(m_1, m_2), \quad \ell = \ell_1 \cdot e^{m_1 - m} + \ell_2 \cdot e^{m_2 - m}.$

Associativity is what enables tiled, parallel computation: independent thread blocks can each produce a $(m, \ell)$ pair on their slice of $x$ and reduce the pairs in any order. We are about to use this to compute attention without ever materializing $S$.

From online softmax to attention

Attention's output is $O_i = \sum_j P_{ij} V_j$. If we knew the row max $m_i$ and the normalizer $\ell_i$ in advance, we could write

$O_i = \frac{1}{\ell_i} \sum_j e^{S_{ij} - m_i} V_j.$

The trick is to maintain a running unnormalized output $\tilde O_i^{(j)} = \sum_{k \le j} e^{S_{ik} - m_i^{(j)}} V_k$ alongside $m_i^{(j)}$ and $\ell_i^{(j)}$. When a new column block arrives and bumps the max, you rescale all three quantities by the same factor $e^{m_i^{(\mathrm{old})} - m_i^{(\mathrm{new})}}$, then add the new contributions. After the last block, divide once: $O_i = \tilde O_i^{(N)} / \ell_i^{(N)}$. The score matrix $S$ never has to exist as a whole.

FlashAttention v1: tiling and recompute

Dao, Fu, Ermon, Rudra, and Ré published FlashAttention at NeurIPS 2022. The forward pass tiles $Q$ into row blocks of size $B_r$ and $K, V$ into column blocks of size $B_c$, with the block sizes chosen so that one block of each fits in SRAM together with the output and softmax statistics. The pseudocode of the inner loop (forward) is:

# Forward pass, simplified.
# Q is (N, d); K, V are (N, d); output O is (N, d); ℓ, m are (N,).
# Outer loop over column blocks of K, V.
for j in range(num_col_blocks):
    K_j = load(K, block=j)            # SRAM tile, shape (B_c, d)
    V_j = load(V, block=j)            # SRAM tile, shape (B_c, d)
    # Inner loop over row blocks of Q.
    for i in range(num_row_blocks):
        Q_i = load(Q, block=i)        # SRAM tile, shape (B_r, d)
        O_i = load(O, block=i)        # current running output
        m_i = load(m, block=i)        # current running max
        l_i = load(l, block=i)        # current running normalizer

        S_ij = Q_i @ K_j.T            # SRAM only: shape (B_r, B_c)
        m_new = max(m_i, rowmax(S_ij))
        P_ij = exp(S_ij - m_new)
        l_new = exp(m_i - m_new) * l_i + rowsum(P_ij)
        O_i  = (l_i * exp(m_i - m_new) / l_new) * O_i \
             + (P_ij @ V_j) / l_new

        store(O, block=i, O_i)
        store(m, block=i, m_new)
        store(l, block=i, l_new)

Two things to notice. First, $S_{ij}$ and $P_{ij}$ are tiny tiles that live entirely in SRAM. Second, the final division by $\ell$ is folded into the running update so $O$ is always normalized correctly at the time of the store. v1's outer loop is over $K, V$ blocks (columns), and the inner loop is over $Q$ blocks (rows). v2 will swap that.

The IO accounting works out as follows. Each outer iteration reads one $K, V$ block ($O(B_c d)$ bytes) and the entire $Q$, $O$, $m$, $\ell$ once. There are $T_c = N / B_c$ outer iterations. The block size satisfies $B_c d \approx M$ where $M$ is SRAM, so $B_c \approx M / d$. Total HBM traffic is therefore $O(T_c \cdot N d) = O(N^2 d^2 / M)$. With $d = 128$ and $M \approx 100$ KB on A100, this is roughly 9× less than standard attention's $O(N^2)$ in practice. Dao et al. prove a matching lower bound: no exact-attention algorithm can do better.

Backward pass: recomputation, not stash

The backward pass needs $P$ to compute gradients of $Q, K, V$. Naive backprop would either store $P$ (spending the IO we just saved) or store $S$ and recompute. FlashAttention v1 stores only the $O(N)$ log-sum-exp vector $L_i = m_i + \log \ell_i$. During backward, each tile recomputes $S_{ij} = Q_i K_j^\top$ from the still-in-HBM $Q, K$, then $P_{ij} = e^{S_{ij} - L_i}$ in one step. Recomputation costs extra FLOPs but saves HBM traffic — and on a memory-bound kernel that is a clear win. This is a recurring theme: spend FLOPs to save bytes.

v1 headline numbers

FlashAttention v1 achieved roughly 124 TFLOP/s on the attention kernel itself and 30–50% of A100 peak FLOPs end-to-end, depending on model. End-to-end training speedups: 15% on BERT-large, 3× on GPT-2 with sequence 1K, 2.4× on Long-Range Arena 1K–4K. It also enabled the first transformers to clear chance accuracy on Path-X (16K tokens) and Path-256 (64K tokens). By replacing $O(N^2)$ HBM with $O(N^2 d^2 / M)$, the wall-clock cost of attention stopped exploding with sequence length.

Advanced From v2 work partitioning to v3 asynchrony

FlashAttention v2: filling the chip

By 2023 the v1 algorithm was a household word in kernel circles, but the implementation was leaving FLOPs on the table. v1 reached only 25–40% of A100's 312 TFLOP/s tensor-core peak on attention even though it was no longer memory-bound. The bottleneck moved up the stack: the kernel was suboptimal in three ways that v2 fixed.

1. Loop reordering. v1's outer loop is over $K, V$ blocks. That means each output row block $O_i$ is read and written multiple times (once per $K, V$ block) within a single thread block. v2 swaps the order: the outer loop becomes $Q$ blocks, the inner loop becomes $K, V$ blocks. Each thread block now owns its $O_i$ entirely; nothing is shared across iterations of the outer loop, so the output row is written exactly once at the end. This also makes the algorithm a textbook map: thread block $i$ handles row block $i$ from start to finish.

2. Sequence-length parallelism. v1 parallelizes over batch and heads. For long context with batch size 1 and a few heads — exactly the inference and long-sequence-training regime — that left most of the GPU's 108 SMs idle. v2 additionally parallelizes over the sequence-length dimension: different SMs handle different $Q$ row blocks of the same head. This recovers occupancy when batch × heads is small.

3. Warp partitioning. Within a thread block, v1 used a "split-K" layout where four warps each held a slice of $K$ and shared $Q$. Producing the row-wise softmax then required cross-warp reductions through shared memory. v2 flips it: warps each hold a slice of $Q$ and share $K, V$. The row reductions become intra-warp. This eliminates a shared-memory round trip for every block.

v2 also reduces non-matmul FLOPs. The original recurrence rescales the running output $\tilde O$ on every iteration. Modern tensor cores execute matmul roughly 16× faster than equivalent FP32 vector arithmetic, so any reduction in non-matmul work is multiplied. v2 defers the rescaling: it keeps $\tilde O$ unnormalized throughout the loop and only divides by $\ell$ at the end. The result is one division per row block instead of one per inner iteration.

Combined, these changes nearly double v1's throughput. v2 hits ~230 TFLOP/s on the attention kernel and 73% of A100 peak, with end-to-end training about 2× faster than v1 and up to 9× faster than vanilla PyTorch. None of this required new hardware — only a more careful kernel.

FlashAttention v3: Hopper, asynchrony, and FP8

NVIDIA's H100 (Hopper) is a different beast. v2 ran on it but only reached ~35% of its 989 TFLOP/s FP16 peak, because Hopper's two big new features — the warp-group matrix-multiply-accumulate (WGMMA) tensor cores and the tensor memory accelerator (TMA) — are asynchronous. WGMMA issues a matmul and returns immediately while the tensor cores chew on it; TMA issues a global-to-shared-memory copy and returns immediately while the DMA engine ferries the bytes. A synchronous v2-style loop wastes those overlap opportunities.

Shah, Bikshandi, Zhang, Thakkar, Ramani, and Dao published FlashAttention-3 in 2024. It reorganizes the kernel around three Hopper-specific ideas:

Warp specialization (producer–consumer). A thread block is split into producer warps and consumer warps. Producers issue TMA loads to bring the next $K, V$ tile into shared memory. Consumers issue WGMMA matmuls on the current tile. The producers run ahead while the consumers compute, and a small ring buffer in shared memory hands tiles between them. This is the GPU equivalent of double buffering generalized to a software pipeline. Producer warps essentially do nothing but call TMA — but TMA takes care of address generation and bounds checking, so the producer warp's only job is to advance pointers and signal mailboxes. This frees up registers in the consumer warps for larger tile sizes.

Ping-pong scheduling. Each row block of $Q$ is processed by two consumer warpgroups that alternate phases. While warpgroup A is doing the $QK^\top$ matmul on tile $j$, warpgroup B is doing the softmax on tile $j-1$. Then they swap: A does softmax on $j$ while B does $QK^\top$ on $j+1$. Hopper's tensor cores can only do matmul; the special function units handle the exponentials and reductions. By overlapping them, the kernel runs softmax in the shadow of the matmuls. This single trick lifts throughput from ~570 TFLOP/s to ~620 TFLOP/s.

Intra-warpgroup pipelining. Even within a single warpgroup, the second matmul ($P V$) can start before the softmax for that tile is fully done, because its inputs become available in pieces. v3 implements a 2-stage pipeline inside the warpgroup that further overlaps softmax with the next matmul. Register pressure goes up, but for FP16 with $d=128$ the kernel still fits and reaches 640–660 TFLOP/s.

FP8 with incoherent processing. Hopper's tensor cores do FP8 matmul at twice the FP16 rate. But quantizing $Q$ and $K$ to FP8 directly is brutal: outliers in attention scores get clipped, accuracy plummets. v3 borrows a trick from QuIP and applies a Hadamard rotation to $Q$ and $K$ before quantization. The rotation spreads outliers across all coordinates so the quantization grid covers the values evenly. Combined with per-block scaling factors, this reduces FP8 quantization error by 2.6× versus naive baselines and runs at ~1.2 PFLOP/s on H100.

End result: v3 hits 740 TFLOP/s in FP16 (75% of H100 peak, vs. 35% for v2 on the same chip) and 1.2 PFLOP/s in FP8. The 2× speedup over v2 is not from a smarter algorithm — it is from rewriting the same algorithm so it actually exploits Hopper's three independent execution streams.

Each version maps to a GPU generation

It is worth pausing on this. v1 is fundamentally an Ampere kernel: synchronous tensor cores, software-managed shared memory, no async copies. v2 is also an Ampere kernel but extracts more parallelism that v1 left on the table. v3 only makes sense on Hopper — try to compile its WGMMA and TMA paths on Ampere and you simply do not have the instructions. The pattern is that each new GPU generation introduces independent execution streams (Volta added tensor cores; Ampere added cp.async; Hopper added WGMMA + TMA), and each Flash Attention version finds a way to overlap them. Blackwell has tensor memory and a new generation of WGMMA; Flash Attention 4 is already in development to exploit it.

Sliding window, sparse patterns, and kernel fusion

Flash Attention's framework extends to many sparsity patterns. Sliding-window attention (each token attends only to the last $W$ tokens) is implemented by skipping outer-loop iterations whose $K, V$ block lies outside the window. Causal masking is the same idea applied to the upper triangle. Block-sparse Flash Attention generalizes to any block-level mask. Because the score tile $S_{ij}$ is only ever materialized in SRAM, the sparsity savings compound: skipped tiles save not just FLOPs but the corresponding SRAM and HBM IO. This is why modern long-context kernels (Mistral's sliding window, GPT's local attention, ALiBi-with-window) are usually written as Flash Attention variants.

The broader lesson is kernel fusion. Standard PyTorch attention is three separate kernels (matmul, softmax, matmul), each of which round-trips through HBM. Fusing them into one kernel collapses the IO. Flash Attention generalizes this from "fuse two matmuls" to "fuse a softmax-in-the-middle by being clever about the recurrence." The same recipe — IO awareness, online algorithms, kernel fusion — has since produced Flash Decoding, fused RoPE, fused MoE expert dispatch, and many more. Reiner's offhand "it doesn't even go to main memory" is exactly this principle in compressed form, and it is the dominant engineering lever in modern LLM inference.

Open questions and what to read next

  • How far can FP8 (and FP4) attention go? v3 shows FP8 attention can match FP16 quality with incoherent processing, but training stability at sub-8-bit precision is largely unknown. What about INT4 attention with rotations?
  • Flash Attention on non-NVIDIA hardware. AMD MI300, Intel Gaudi, Google TPU, and MatX's chip all need the same algorithmic ideas but different kernel implementations. How do the loop ordering and warp partitioning translate to systolic-array architectures?
  • Decode-time attention. Flash Decoding (Dao et al., 2023) parallelizes the inner loop over $K, V$ blocks for the single-query case. Can we do better when KV cache size dwarfs the query?
  • Approximate attention with the same kernel. Block-sparse, top-k, and learned sparsity all want to slot into the Flash Attention loop. The hardest part is keeping the score tile decision dynamic without a host-device round trip.
  • Compiler synthesis. Triton, ThunderKittens, and CUTLASS each let you express Flash-Attention-shaped kernels at higher abstraction. Can a compiler produce v3-quality kernels automatically across architectures?
